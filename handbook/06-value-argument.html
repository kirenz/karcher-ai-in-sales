37  Value Argumentation

Turn the confirmed need profile from discovery into a customer-specific value argument: a four-step value chain that ties every product fact to a need the customer actually confirmed, prioritised by what they weighted, with total cost of ownership as the bridge from price to business value.

On the map above, the customer is still in Evaluation, now asking whether your answer is worth it. You turn the confirmed need into a customer-specific value case.

This is Step 6 of the sales cycle, and it is where the whole thread so far pays off. You walked out of the needs analysis with one artifact that steers everything from here: a confirmed need profile, the customer’s answers tagged into the six dimensions, with the strong needs evidenced and the thin ones named. Now you turn that profile into a value argument the customer recognises as their own situation, not a product pitch that drifts straight to price. The input is the need profile, not the catalogue. The output is one customer-specific value block for NorthStar, ready to become a presentation in the next step.

37.1 Why features don’t sell and value does

A list of features does not win a deal. You can describe the machine well, state every specification correctly, word it nicely, and the customer will still not feel why the offer matters to their situation. Worse, a pitch that leads with the product sounds like every other vendor, and the conversation drifts straight to price. A product fact only becomes value when you tie it to a need the customer has confirmed. That single link is the whole job of this step.

This matters especially in an account like NorthStar. Service continuity, onboarding quality, and rollout logic carry real commercial weight there. If the conversation starts with equipment alone, those drivers disappear too early, and procurement gets an easy path to reduce everything to unit price, because the broader business logic was never made visible. The objective is not to make you more aggressive or more defensive. It is to make the commercial logic clearer and to lead with what discovery already proved the customer cares about.

37.2 The hypothesis from research becomes the argument

Back in the research step you formed a first value hypothesis about why Kärcher might matter to NorthStar — uptime, service continuity, a low-risk pilot. That was an educated guess that shaped the first email and the discovery questions. In the needs analysis the customer confirmed which of those needs are real. So the argument you build now is no longer a guess; it is a substantiated case, grounded in what the customer actually said.

That arc — hypothesis to confirmed need to proven argument — is what makes this value block credible rather than generic. Every benefit you claim should trace back along that line. If a benefit has no root in the confirmed need profile, it is a feature claim wearing the costume of an argument, and a competitor can match it just as easily.

37.3 Start from the need profile, not the catalogue

The value argument is only as good as the discovery behind it. You build value from what the customer confirmed, not from what the brochure lists. In the NorthStar case, discovery surfaced a few well-evidenced dimensions. The strongest signals sit in Service (response time and continuity across sites), Performance (consistent cleaning results across a fragmented fleet), and Economy (procurement will test whether the service component is justified). So the value story should lead with continuity, reliable results, and a defensible cost logic. Anything that drifts back into generic product claims weakens the logic immediately.

The practical test for every line is simple: can the customer hear their own situation inside the benefit? If yes, the value chain is working. If no, the argument is generic and needs sharpening. The need profile is the filter that makes the argument customer-specific, and it is what protects you from improvising benefits that sound reasonable but trace back to nothing the customer actually said. Same machines, same scope — but told from the confirmed need, the offer reflects the customer’s own situation back to them instead of sounding like every other supplier.

37.4 A value chain in four plain steps

The structure behind a strong argument is four plain steps. You do not need to name the framework in front of the customer, and you do not need to drill the acronym to use it. In the background it runs Feature, Advantage, Benefit, Check (FABC).

  1. Feature — what the offer is.
  2. Advantage — what that feature lets the customer do.
  3. Benefit — why that matters to this customer, the explicit value and profit link.
  4. Check — a question that confirms the value actually landed.

It helps to see the four steps as two halves. The first half describes the offer: the feature is the fact, the advantage is the capability it unlocks. Most sales arguments live entirely here, and that is exactly why they feel like a product pitch — the customer can get all of it from any brochure. The second half is where value is created. The benefit makes the link to this customer explicit, and the check is a question that finds out whether the value actually landed instead of assuming it did.

The benefit step is the real centre of gravity, and it is the one most arguments skip. They stop after the feature and the advantage and never translate the offer into customer value. The benefit only works if it points back to a need the customer confirmed and stays in the customer’s own language. If you cannot trace a benefit to something the customer said, do not claim it. The check question matters just as much, because it keeps the exchange a two-way conversation rather than a monologue. When you build a chain, spend your attention on the second half. The first half is easy.

37.5 What a usable NorthStar value chain looks like

Here is one complete value chain for NorthStar, written for Lars Meijer, the Regional Operations Director, on the three-unit scrubber-drier pilot. Lead with uptime, because that is what Lars confirmed he needs.

  • Feature — three Kärcher BR 45/22 C Bp Pack scrubber driers plus onboarding and a service-start package. This is deliberately more than the machine, because the customer is buying a working operation, not just three machines.
  • Advantage — a faster pilot ramp-up with built-in service continuity, not only raw cleaning power.
  • Benefit — less disruption during critical cleaning windows across sites, and lower risk as the pilot moves toward a broader rollout decision.
  • Check — “How important is uninterrupted uptime for this pilot?” — which makes the conversation interactive again and invites confirmation or correction.
TipRead the chain backwards to test it

Every line traces back to something discovery confirmed. The feature includes the service deliberately, the advantage names what that combination enables, the benefit connects straight to the strongest need in the profile, and the check keeps Lars in the conversation. If you read the benefit and cannot point to the discovery note behind it, the chain is not ready — that line is a guess, not an argument.

37.6 Prioritise by the needs the customer confirmed

Not every true benefit deserves equal airtime. Discovery did not just confirm needs, it ranked them across the six dimensions (Service, Performance, Economy, Image, Comfort, Safety). So the value argument should be ordered the same way. Lead with the dimension the customer weighted highest and could evidence most clearly, and let the marginal points fall to the bottom or drop out entirely.

For NorthStar, that means service continuity and reliable performance lead, the economy question follows because procurement will test it, and anything you cannot tie back to a stated priority does not make the cut. Prioritising like this keeps the argument short, honest, and aimed at what the customer actually decided on. A value chain with three sharp, well-evidenced links beats one with eight links where half are guesses.

37.7 Match the language to the role

One account usually needs more than one kind of benefit language, because the people in the Buying Center listen for different value. The commercial core stays stable; only the emphasis changes. The mistake to avoid is not using both lenses — it is mixing them carelessly: pitching a strategic cost case to a site supervisor, or usability language to procurement.

Lars Meijer — Operations Sophie Verbeek — Procurement
What they buy Uptime and continuity A defensible number
What they listen for Operations that just work Predictable, comparable cost
Benefit line Less disruption across sites A defensible total-cost-of-ownership case per site

The difference is what each one is really doing. Lars is running an operation, so he buys uptime and continuity. Sophie is not buying uptime — she is defending a number in an approval meeting, so she needs predictable cost and a value case that survives a price comparison, not just a low unit price. Both lenses can be true at once: keep the commercial core stable and shift only the framing to fit the role.

37.8 TCO: the bridge from price to business value

Total cost of ownership (TCO) — the cost over the whole life of the equipment, not just the purchase price — is the strongest bridge from a price discussion to a business-value discussion. When the conversation drifts toward unit price, the answer is not to avoid commercial clarity. It is to improve it by moving from purchase price to total operating cost over time.

A unit-price comparison hides the cost of downtime, slow ramp-up, and a fragmented fleet that nobody can service consistently. The total-cost framing makes those costs visible, so service responsiveness, onboarding, and uptime stop looking like decorative add-ons and start looking like what they are: real drivers of what the pilot costs to run. This is where you quantify the value wherever you can, because a number the customer recognises is harder to argue away than an adjective.

WarningTwo cautions on the numbers

Do not invent false precision, and treat every figure as a draft until it is verified against the case material. The aim is to connect the service component to operating conditions the customer already cares about — uptime, continuity, and the cost of implementation friction — so the offer stays comparable without collapsing into a machine-only debate. A TCO number the customer cannot follow does not persuade them; it makes them suspicious.

37.9 Where the product facts come from: the YelloFile

You do not feed the Gem product facts from memory, and you do not let it improvise them. The approved source is the YelloFile, Kärcher’s internal product factsheet for the machine — for this pilot, the YelloFile for the BR 45/22 C Bp Pack. You attach it alongside the need profile, and the Gem reads the real specifications from it instead of guessing.

A YelloFile is more than a spec table. Beside the technical data (working width, area performance, runtime) it already carries the argument: the customer-benefit points and the total-cost logic the product was built on. For the BR 45/22 that is a single battery instead of two, fewer service cases and a better total cost of ownership, a three-year battery warranty, and the manoeuvrability of the turnable brush head. That is exactly the Advantage and Benefit raw material for your chains, straight from the source rather than from memory.

But the rule from earlier still holds: the confirmed need is the filter, not the YelloFile. You pull only the facts and benefit points that map to a need the customer confirmed, and you leave the rest of the catalogue out. A YelloFile full of true features is still a catalogue until the need profile decides which of them matter here.

WarningThe YelloFile is internal — keep it internal

A YelloFile is approved product material, but it is built for internal use: it carries net and list prices, part numbers, availability, and competitor data. Use it as your source, but never carry prices, part numbers, or competitor figures into the customer-facing argument. Keep the internal source and the customer-facing argument separate, and verify the draft before you send.

37.10 The Value-Argument Gem drafts the block

The Gem for this step is the Value-Argument Gem (“Value-Argument (Kärcher Sales)”), and it lives right in Google Docs beside your work. It is a saved assistant set up once with the FABC structure, a product-to-benefit logic, a TCO framing template, and the role-benefit language for operations and procurement. You build it once and reuse it on every account.

Open the Gem and attach two things: your NorthStar need-profile Doc (plus any draft proposal notes) and the YelloFile for the product, then prompt it. The need profile decides what the argument is about; the YelloFile gives the Gem the real product facts and benefit points. The Gem takes the confirmed needs and builds the Feature–Advantage–Benefit–Check chains, ordered by the priorities the customer confirmed. It then gives you two role variants in one pass — an operations version for Lars and a procurement/TCO version for Sophie — and adds a total-cost line where one applies. In under a minute you have two role-specific drafts grounded in the same need profile. It is a self-contained Google Doc — nothing to connect or sync.

You normally just open the prepared Gem, attach your need-profile Doc and the YelloFile, and name the offer and the roles.

37.11 You own the quality: three checks before it ships

The Gem drafts fast, but the salesperson owns the quality. That comes down to three checks you run yourself — the same quality gate from the foundations module applied to the value block.

  1. Trace — walk down the chain and ask whether each benefit maps to a need the customer actually confirmed, or whether the model invented something plausible. If a line has no root in discovery, cut it.
  2. Verify — every figure is a draft, especially the total-cost numbers, so check them against the case material before anything reaches the customer. Remove any claim you cannot support.
  3. Sound — read the check question aloud and ask whether it sounds like a real human question or like a form. If it reads like a survey, rewrite it.

Because a customer-facing proposal needs sign-off, the model proposes and you approve. The Gem produces the first draft; you decide what is true and what to send, and you verify it before it reaches the customer, in line with the Module 0 quality gate.

37.13 In summary

This step turns the confirmed need profile into customer-relevant value. A four-step value chain (Feature, Advantage, Benefit, Check) connects every product fact to a need the customer actually confirmed, with the framework kept in the background. The value hypothesis you first formed in research becomes a substantiated argument here, because the real need is now known. Because the NorthStar Buying Center weights value differently, the benefit language shifts by role: operational benefit such as uptime for Lars, commercial benefit such as a defensible cost case for Sophie. Total-cost-of-ownership framing bridges from price to business value, and you quantify where you can while treating every figure as a draft to verify. The Value-Argument Gem drafts the block and the role variants inside Google Docs — a standalone document — but the salesperson checks the evidence and owns the decision.

The output is one customer-specific value block for NorthStar, ready to feed the presentation in the next module. Objection handling, including Sophie’s price objection, belongs with the closing step, where it is handled properly, so it is intentionally left out here.

NoteWhat the research says

The chapter’s central move — lead with value, not features — is one of the better-evidenced ideas in modern selling research. Terho, Haas, Eggert and Ulaga (2012) conceptualised value-based selling as crafting the sales effort around the customer’s business value rather than the product — vivid enough that they called it “taking the sales out of selling.” And it is not just a posture. Terho, Eggert, Haas and Ulaga (2015) found that sales strategy only reaches performance when it is carried by behaviour, and value-based selling is one of the two behaviours that carry it. (The other, customer orientation, was the engine of the discovery step before this one.) This is the step where that behaviour does its visible work: an argument built on a need the customer actually confirmed is value-based selling in practice, which is why it leans on the profile discovery confirmed.

How you construct the argument is the most useful piece of evidence here. Anderson, Narus and van Rossum (2006) distinguish three kinds of value proposition. The weakest simply lists all benefits — which invites benefit assertion, claiming advantages for features the customer does not care about. Better is favourable points of difference against the next alternative. The strongest is resonating focus: leading with the one or two points that matter most to this customer, evidenced and in their language. That is the academic root of two disciplines in this chapter — the FABC test “can the customer hear their own situation in the benefit?” and the instruction to prioritise by the dimensions the customer weighted. A short, focused argument is not a simplification; it is the better argument.

Quantifying value has its own literature. Anderson, Kumar and Narus (2007), in Value Merchants, argue that suppliers should demonstrate and document value rather than assert it — translating a difference in performance into a monetary figure built from the customer’s own operating data. That is the substance behind “quantify where you can,” and it is why a documented number outperforms an adjective. The discipline that must travel with it: no false precision, every figure a draft until verified.

For the price-to-value bridge, total cost of ownership is the canonical tool. Ellram (1995) defined the TCO method from the purchasing side — looking beyond price to the many other costs of a purchase across its life — which is precisely Sophie’s logic in procurement. But the honest scope matters: Wouters, Anderson and Wynstra (2005) found that buyers adopt TCO only when its perceived value is clear and the data is available, because it is information- and effort-intensive. A vendor-supplied TCO case persuades only when it is transparent and credible — which is exactly why the “verify the numbers” rule is not optional.

ImportantValue-based selling is harder than it sounds

The most useful counter-evidence: value-based selling is not a quick technique. Töytäri and Rajala (2015) show it is an organisational capability — a value-based mindset, the tools to quantify value, aligned incentives, and a customer willing to engage. Where that capability is missing, firms slip back into product-and-price selling despite their intentions (the “value-selling gap”). The practical reading for this step: the Gem drafts the block in seconds, but the part that actually wins — tracing each benefit to a confirmed need and standing behind every number — is human work that no tool removes.

NoteWhen everyone can draft a value argument

Generative AI now lets any rep produce benefit language in seconds, which quietly raises the bar rather than lowering it. When fluent value claims are cheap, fluency stops being the differentiator. What stays scarce is the discipline the research points to: resonating focus over a list of benefits, a number you can defend over an adjective, and a benefit that traces to something the customer actually said. The Gem makes the draft fast; that only makes the human judgement more decisive, not less.
