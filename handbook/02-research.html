34  Research & Qualification

Turn a raw lead into a sourced briefing and an Go or No-Go, built with a prepared Gem. Along the way you write the first value hypothesis, the thread that runs through the rest of the cycle.

On the map above, the customer is entering Consideration and beginning to ask “what’s out there to help?”. Before you reach out, you work out whether this account is worth your time, and why Kärcher might matter.

A lead has landed, but right now it is only a company name and a hunch. The job of this step is to turn that raw lead into two things: a briefing you could walk into a meeting with, and an explicit decision about whether the account is worth your time at all. Out of that research comes the first answer to “why might Kärcher matter to this customer?” That answer is your first value hypothesis, the thread you carry through the rest of the cycle, and it starts as a sentence you write here.

  • A prepared Gem with Deep Research does the finding and returns a structured eight-part briefing; your effort goes to judgment, not gathering.
  • The briefing’s Kärcher fit section is your first value hypothesis — why Kärcher might matter here — the thread you carry through the whole cycle.

34.1 From a blank page to a briefing and a decision

The shift in this step is from gathering to deciding. The old way is to open a dozen tabs and hope a picture forms, and then still judge whether the account is worth it. The AI way is to ask for a sourced first briefing in minutes, so your effort goes to the two things only you can do: verifying the claims that matter, and making the call.

The tool that does the finding is Deep Research, an AI mode that searches many public sources and cites them, so the report comes back structured and traceable. You do not learn Deep Research in detail today. You learn to drive one prepared Gem that has it switched on.

34.2 The Account-Briefing Gem does the research for you

Rather than writing a long prompt from scratch every time, this step uses a prepared Gem: a saved AI expert you set up once with Kärcher context. This one is the Account-Briefing Gem, and it already holds our ideal customer profile (with the priority segments and scope set per market), what Kärcher Professional sells and how we talk about service, a briefing template, and a short BANT guide. Deep Research is on by default.

One thing about that context: part is universal, part is local. The Kärcher fit logic is built in for everyone; the market-specific facts (which segments are priority where you sell, what size of account is worth a pilot, the competitors you meet) are set once for your market by your local sales team. So if the Gem flags an assumption or asks a short question about your market, that is by design, not a gap.

The Gem is built and maintained centrally and shared with you read-only, so everyone works from the same structure. The workflow is deliberately short: open the Gem, give it the company name and its website, get a structured, sourced briefing plus a first Go/No-Go.

It returns one consistent structure every time, so nothing important gets skipped:

# Section What it gives you
1 Company snapshot Size, footprint, business model
2 Sites & operations Where cleaning, hygiene, and uptime matter
3 Pain & need hypotheses Likely pressure on cost, downtime, standardization, safety
4 Buying signals & triggers Recent moves, growth, tenders, timing
5 Competitive / incumbent context Who is already supplying them
6 Kärcher fit The equipment + service + standardization angle (the first value hypothesis)
7 Open questions What to verify in the meeting
8 Qualification (BANT) + Go/No-Go An explicit recommendation, with reasons

Two sections carry a flag. The Pain & need and Kärcher fit sections are hypotheses, not facts, and the Gem marks them that way. Open questions is gold: it hands you the gaps public sources could not close and turns them into questions you ask the customer live.

Prompts you can copy and try. You supply only the company and its website, and optionally the segment you sell into or a source you already trust. You never describe the account’s pains, fit, or Go/No-Go yourself — that is exactly what the Gem researches and drafts. One note on the company: the prompts here point at ISS (issworld.com), a real facility-services company, because a live research run needs a real company to research — NorthStar is fictional (more on that just below).

Start with the simplest version: a company and its website. This is the example prompt the Gem itself suggests:

Format: Return the briefing in exactly this eight-part structure, so nothing important is skipped.
By default keep it to a focused briefing of about 1,000–1,500 words; expand only on request.

     assumptions, and a one-line research plan.
  2. Company snapshot — size, footprint, business model.
  3. Sites & operations — where cleaning, hygiene, and uptime actually matter for this company.
  4. Pain & need hypotheses — likely pressure on cost, downtime, standardization, safety (label as

  5. Buying signals & triggers — recent moves, growth, new sites, tenders, timing.

     would flip the call.
Use inline citations on every central claim, figure, signal, and contested point; the source list is
not a substitute, so close with one (links and publication dates). Tag each central claim with
evidence type (official filing or statistic / regulation or standard / analyst or trade report /
operator case / expert view / inference), a confidence level (high / medium / low), and a one-line
reason. Plain, internal, analyst tone: no marketing buzzwords, no pitch. Respond in the same language
the salesperson writes in.

Check:
- Separate established fact, evidence-backed pattern, plausible inference, and to-verify claim,
  and mark sections 4 and 7 and the BANT read as hypotheses. Ground every figure in a named, dated
  source; mark a useful-but-unsourced, stale, ambiguous, or salesperson-supplied number
  "to-verify" rather than stating it. When a fact is not findable, say so plainly instead of
  filling the gap.
- Lead with the business outcome, not the machine: name a product only as a possible means to an
  outcome, never as the headline, and only when an attached Kärcher-approved source supports it.
- If the salesperson gave no market context, assess fit qualitatively, state any market assumption

  the Go/No-Go genuinely hinges on a market fact you lack. Don't open with a questionnaire.

  result, say so and draft only an outline. End on a decision and a short list of sharp open
  questions, not a tidy summary.
- Reserve a hard stop for what is costly if wrong: never present invented statistics, internal
  numbers, contracts, prices, or contact names as fact, and never reproduce confidential customer
  or account data in the output.

34.3 Kärcher fit is your first value hypothesis

This is the most important idea in the chapter. The briefing’s Kärcher fit section answers one question: why might Kärcher matter here? Given this account’s operation, where could equipment, service, and standardization actually help them, in business terms, not product terms. The answer you write there is the first value hypothesis.

Name it out loud, because this hypothesis is the thread that runs through the rest of the training. It shapes the first email in the next step, so the message speaks to a real reason rather than a generic pitch. It shapes the discovery questions in Step 5, where you finally get to test it with the customer. And once the need is confirmed, it becomes the substantiated value argument in Step 6. Right now it is only a hypothesis built from public information, which is exactly why verifying it matters so much.

34.4 See it work: ISS live, NorthStar prepared

Here is the convention in full, so the two names never mix up. NorthStar Facility Services is the account we work across the whole training, but it is fictional, so there is nothing real to research: you cannot run Deep Research on a company that does not exist. That is why every live prompt in this chapter points at ISS (issworld.com), a real facility-services and contract-cleaning company in the same industry — the one live demonstration of what a sourced briefing looks like. ISS is only the stand-in; NorthStar stays the account we actually work.

This is the live moment of the chapter. You only name the account; the Gem carries the structure, runs Deep Research, and takes the briefing through to a BANT read and a Go/No-Go.

Notice the speed: by hand this is an afternoon of work. We do not build on ISS, though; we take the same Gem to NorthStar. Because NorthStar is fictional, we prepared its result in advance, the same eight-part briefing run on its public website, through to a BANT read and a Go/No-Go. Two parts carry the most weight. Kärcher fit is the first value hypothesis: not a machine pitch but a service-backed standardization story around uptime, mapped onto the pilot of three BR 45/22 scrubber driers. The final qualification section ends in the call, a Go, with the one or two facts (an incumbent contract, or no pilot budget) that would flip it. Every claim is labelled verified or to-verify, so you know exactly what to check.

1. Company snapshot

  • Company: NorthStar Facility Services Benelux (verified)
  • HQ: Rotterdam, Netherlands (Waalhaven Eastside) (verified)
  • Footprint: Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg (verified)
  • Business model: Industrial facility services and contract cleaning; a regional operator with a multi-site delivery model (verified)
  • Positioning: “Services for industrial pace, not generic facility volume” — service-led and uptime-oriented (verified)

2. Sites & operations

NorthStar publicly states three customer environments where downtime and inconsistent routines create immediate pressure (verified):

  • Industrial production — manufacturing and process sites, where downtime or uneven cleaning quality hits output and readiness.
  • Logistics — distribution and throughput hubs that need repeatable routines across shifts.
  • Multi-site portfolios — regional networks that want one operating model instead of local workarounds.

Operations run from a Rotterdam coordination hub with regional, site-level execution, organized around shift-critical operating windows (verified).

3. Pain & need hypotheses (hypotheses from public signals, not confirmed)

  • Mid-shift downtime — the site repeatedly stresses uptime and shift-change readiness, which suggests equipment failures mid-shift are a felt pain. (inference)
  • Variation across teams — heavy “standard work” language implies inconsistent local routines they want to remove. (inference)
  • Onboarding speed — operator ramp-up is framed as part of service quality, so slow onboarding likely costs them. (inference)
  • Escalation & continuity — repeated continuity and escalation language implies service gaps after launch. (inference)

4. Buying signals & triggers

  • Recent public updates (March 2026): expanding industrial contract coverage; a “standard-work refresh” for shift-change cleaning windows; the Rotterdam team strengthening new-site launch support (verified — public news). Together these point to active growth and appetite for more standardized routines.
  • Pilot-to-rollout logic is stated openly: prove one site, then scale (verified). A small first scope is realistic.
  • Tender route is public (a dedicated Tender Desk), so procurement will formally review scope and price (verified).

5. Competitive / incumbent context

  • No public signal. The site names no current cleaning-equipment supplier, fleet brand, or competitor. Who supplies them today is to verify in conversation. (not findable — do not assume)

6. Kärcher fit — first value hypothesis (hypothesis)

Given the public picture, the strongest Kärcher angle is not a machine pitch but a service-backed standardization story around uptime:

  • Reliable battery scrubber driers that cut mid-shift breakdowns answer the uptime language directly.
  • One standardized fleet plus service supports their “one operating model, not local workarounds” theme.
  • Operator onboarding and service continuity (a Kärcher strength) sit inside the value story, not beside it.

This maps cleanly onto the pilot of three Kärcher BR 45/22 C Bp Pack scrubber driers: prove uptime and standardization on one cluster, then scale with the rollout logic NorthStar already favors. (A hypothesis to test in discovery, not a confirmed need.)

7. Open questions (to ask the customer live)

  • Which site is the most credible pilot location right now?
  • What scrubber driers are in use today, and where exactly do they fail mid-shift?
  • Which pressure windows hurt most: shift changes, incidents, or high-throughput periods?
  • Who owns the operational decision, and who owns commercial approval?
  • What pilot result would justify a broader rollout?
  • Which service elements matter most: onboarding, response time, maintenance continuity, or escalation ownership?

8. Qualification (BANT) + Go / No-Go

BANT is a working hypothesis from public information, to confirm in the discovery conversation (Step 5), never a verdict:

  • Budget — to verify. No public budget signal; the visible Tender Desk implies a formal procurement process. (inference)
  • Authority — the public contact structure already hints at a buying center: operations (Lars Meijer, Regional Operations Director), procurement (Sophie Verbeek, Procurement Manager), site (Milan Bakker), and HSE / service (Eva van Dijk). Operational and commercial approval clearly sit with different people. (verified — public contacts)
  • Need — plausible and well-aligned (uptime, standardization, continuity), but inferred from public language, not confirmed. (hypothesis)
  • Timing — pilot-first logic and recent growth suggest openness to a small pilot now; no concrete timing is public. (inference)

Recommendation: GO (qualify further). The public fit is strong: an industrial / logistics profile, explicit uptime and standardization language, a stated pilot-to-rollout model, and a visible multi-stakeholder buying center. The account is worth real preparation time.

What would flip it to No-Go: a locked-in contract with an incumbent equipment supplier, or no budget / timing for a pilot this cycle. Both are unknown from public sources, so confirm them early.

Limits

  • Public website material only. It shows positioning and likely priorities, not confirmed buying intent.
  • It does not confirm budget, current fleet details, competitor presence, contract timing, or internal approval logic.
  • Everything marked hypothesis, inference, or to-verify must be checked with the customer before it drives a commitment.

34.5 Treat it as a hypothesis, verify what matters

Confident is not the same as verified. This is the discipline that keeps AI trustworthy in sales: a generative model can sound completely confident and still be wrong, so the Gem cites its sources and marks every claim as verified or to-verify, and your job is to use those labels.

Verified To verify
Backed by a public source Inferred or unsupported
Has a source link you can open Check before the meeting
Safe to build on Ask the customer live

So you check the two or three to-verify claims that really matter before the meeting, and turn the rest into questions for the customer. The habit is small and it is the whole game: cite, label, check what matters. No evidence, no send.

The same caution applies to the qualification. BANT asks whether there is a Budget, who has the Authority to decide, whether there is a real Need, and what the Timing is. The Gem gives a first BANT read from public information, useful for the Go or No-Go, but from the outside you only see hints.

What public info hints at What only the conversation confirms
A plausible budget range The real budget
Likely decision-makers Who actually signs
A probable need and timing The need in their own words

Those only become solid in the discovery conversation, Step 5. So BANT is a working hypothesis, never a verdict, and treating it that way keeps you from over-committing on a guess.

34.6 The point of all this: Go or No-Go

All of this research earns its keep in one decision. The briefing ends with an explicit recommendation, and you make the real call.

  • Go — pursue, and carry the open questions forward into first contact and discovery.
  • No-Go — park it, but never silently. Write down why, because a parked account with a clear reason can be revisited when something changes.
  • Name the one or two things that would flip the call: the missing fact or the trigger that would turn a No-Go into a Go.

A qualification without a decision is just a nicely formatted summary. The decision is the deliverable.

NoteWhat the research says

The instinct behind this chapter, that research and qualification are a real stage of selling and not just busywork, is exactly how the discipline has always been framed. In the classic account of the sales process, the work before the conversation, finding and preparing for the right prospect, is its own step, not an afterthought (Moncrief & Marshall 2005). What AI changes is who does the heavy lifting inside that step.

The healthiest way to think about that is collaborative intelligence: along the B2B sales funnel, the machine contributes data gathering and pattern-finding while the human contributes judgment, context, and relationship (Paschen et al. 2020). That is the division of labour this chapter builds in, and it matches how the field as a whole reads AI in selling, as augmentation that raises the work rather than replacing the seller’s judgment (Singh et al. 2019; Grewal et al. 2024). The same research is candid about the catch: generative tools have real limits on factual reliability and control, which is precisely why the verify habit is not optional.

That catch has a name. Generative language models hallucinate: they produce fluent, confident text that is not always faithful to any source (Ji et al. 2023). Citing sources and grounding the answer in retrieved evidence reduces the problem, but it does not remove it, so a human still checks. This is the evidence behind “confident is not the same as verified.” A related finding from sales specifically: people only trust and use a model’s output when they can see why it says what it says, so explainability, not just accuracy, is what makes an AI recommendation usable (Bohanec et al. 2017). The Gem’s cite-and-label behaviour is built for exactly that.

On qualifying and prioritizing, the research backs the “not every lead deserves your afternoon” stance. Model-supported prospect prediction has been used for years to rank B2B leads by their likely value (D’Haen & Van den Poel 2013), and more recent work sharpens the order of operations: you profile before you score, because a good priority call rests on good research first (Wu et al. 2026). That is the academic shape of this whole chapter. As for BANT, it remains widely used, around half of reps still rely on it, but practitioners are clear that in modern, multi-stakeholder buying it is a starting hypothesis, not a checklist that decides the deal, since budget and the real decision-makers often only emerge during the process (Salesforce / Gartner 2024). Holding BANT as a hypothesis is the evidence-based move, not a compromise.

NoteA market signal, not a gimmick

This is not a fringe use of AI. In B2B sales, the top use cases for generative AI are prospecting and lead generation, and the single most sought-after one is a “smart research assistant”, precisely the account-briefing job in this chapter. The estimated productivity prize across marketing and sales runs to USD 0.8–1.2 trillion, and most commercial leaders expect to use these tools routinely within a couple of years (McKinsey 2023). The skill you practise here is the one the market is already moving toward.
