34  Contacts & First Contact

Turn the research brief into a map of who actually decides, then write one first email built for a single job: winning the meeting, not closing the deal.

On the map above, you are still in the customer’s Consideration stage. Several people will shape this decision; your job is to reach the right one and earn a first conversation.

This is Step 3 of the sales cycle, and it is the point where preparation turns into contact. You arrive with two things from the research step: the NorthStar account brief and the first value hypothesis, the early answer to why Kärcher might matter to this customer.

The question now is simple and concrete: who exactly do you reach, and how do you open in a way that earns a reply? The value-hypothesis thread we follow through the whole training lands here for the first time in a real message, and it lands first with the decision-maker.

  • In B2B you sell to a Buying Center: several people who each judge the same offer by a different question (outcome, cost and risk, daily work).
  • Map the stakeholders before you write, straight from the research brief — every entry a hypothesis to verify, never an invented person.
  • The first email is a door opener, not a proposal: built on Purpose / Benefit / Check and the value hypothesis, one real pressure, no specs, one small next step.
  • Match tone to role, not personality — reading the individual live (DISC) is the next chapter.

34.1 You sell to people, not to a company

In B2B you almost never sell to one decision-maker. A purchase like a fleet of cleaning machines is shaped by several people: an operations lead, a procurement manager, the crew on the floor who run the machines. Each weighs the same offer differently, so the classic mistake is to write one generic message to the average of them all, which fits none of them. The skill you build here is to map those people before the meeting, using the raw material the research step already gave you. The goal is modest on purpose: not to close anything, but to win the first conversation.

The Buying Center is simply everyone who influences a buying decision. Three roles carry most of the weight, and each judges the offer by a different question:

  • Decision-maker — judges it by the business outcome for the operation: less downtime, a clean rollout, something that scales. Often an operations or business lead.
  • Purchasing — judges it by cost, scope, and risk: is the deal defendable internally?
  • User — runs the machine day to day, judges it by whether it makes their daily work easier or harder under pressure.

Same offer, three different questions. The decision-maker thinks in outcomes; purchasing and the floor want proof. None of this is guesswork about personality — it is role logic, the same for every account you will ever work, which is exactly why a Gem can prepare it for you in minutes.

34.2 The four people behind NorthStar

Theory is easy to nod along to and hard to use, so we make it concrete. NorthStar has four people in its Buying Center, and across the rest of the training you will work with them by name, not as abstract roles. Lars and Sophie are the primary contacts; Milan and Eva are the operational voices who matter especially in the needs analysis. Treat everything here as a working hypothesis from the briefing, not as confirmed fact.

Lars Meijer, Regional Operations Director — the operational sponsor. The decision-maker who creates forward motion, and your first serious contact. He owns operational continuity across the region, so his question is always about outcome: does this reduce downtime without adding complexity, and can it scale beyond one site? He is where the value hypothesis lands first, because it is written in his language: less disruption, more continuity. Angle: impact and outcome.

Sophie Verbeek, Procurement Manager — the commercial gatekeeper. She holds the formal sign-off and tests the deal: is the service component justified, is the scope clear, can the offer be defended internally? To her, an unexplained service line just reads as a higher price. She is not the first person you write to, but a sponsor who loves the solution still needs procurement to say yes. Angle: cost and risk.

Milan Bakker, Site Supervisor — the daily user. Closest to the machines. A unit that fails mid-shift is his problem; a machine awkward to operate slows his crew down. His question is simple: will this be reliable and easy to use under pressure? He may not sign, but his voice carries weight, because sponsor and procurement both look to the people who run the equipment. Angle: the daily work.

Eva van Dijk, HSE and Service Lead — the safe-rollout voice. Secondary here, but not unimportant. She cares about safe rollout, service response time, and training quality. She rarely champions a deal but can quietly stall one with a late safety or training concern, so you keep her in view. She returns properly in the needs analysis. Angle: safe rollout, response time, training.

34.3 What each role really cares about

The relevance map is the whole point of the Buying Center. The same NorthStar pilot looks attractive for a different reason to each role, and your message has to match the reason, not the average:

Role Person The question they ask What you lead with
Decision-maker Lars Meijer Does this reduce downtime and scale cleanly? Impact and outcome
Purchasing Sophie Verbeek Is the cost defendable and the scope clear? Cost and risk
User Milan Bakker Is it reliable and usable under pressure? The daily work
Safe-rollout voice Eva van Dijk Are response time and training handled? Safe rollout

This is information you already hold from the research step — you are matching role logic, not guessing at personalities.

34.4 Map the stakeholders with the Buying-Center Gem

You do not build the stakeholder map by hand from a blank page. The Buying-Center Gem (“Buying-Center (Kärcher Sales)”) is a prepared Gemini expert that already knows the Buying Center logic. You hand it the account brief from the research step, and it returns a map: for each likely role, a goal, a pain, a conversation angle, and the risk if you ignore that person.

  • Input — the NorthStar account brief from the research step.
  • Output — a role-by-role map: role · goal · pain · conversation angle · risk.
  • Check — every entry flagged as a hypothesis to verify in the meeting; no invented named people.

You normally just open the prepared Gem and attach your account-briefing Doc; if anything it needs is missing, it asks before it maps rather than inventing it.

Prompts you can copy and try. You never describe the roles or angles yourself — that is exactly what the Gem maps. You only attach your briefing Doc and, if you like, point it at one account or ask it to focus a particular way.

The standard move is to attach your account-briefing Doc from Step 2, then ask. This is the example prompt the Gem itself suggests:

TipCopy into the Buying-Center Gem 
Using the account briefing I attached, map the Buying Center for a Kärcher Professional pilot at this account.

Shortest version: attach the Doc and just ask for the map. It reads the brief and states any assumption it had to make:

Once you have the map, ask the Gem to turn it into an approach plan, one move per role (you can also name the account explicitly, or have it go deeper on a single role):

TipNo names in the brief? Map roles, not people

Often the research turns up no individuals at all, and that is normal — it is not a gap the Gem should fill by inventing someone. You still map the roles: a mid-size operator has someone who owns uptime, someone who owns the budget, and people who run the machines, whether or not you have their names yet. The map stays a set of role hypotheses. The names come from elsewhere — a LinkedIn title search, a quick call to the site (“who looks after your cleaning equipment?”), or the first conversation itself, where you ask who else should be in the room. Write to the most likely role, and use that first contact to find the real people behind it.

When the map comes back, run the human check, because a confident-looking map is seductive. Read each entry and ask one question per person: what is the single thing I could ask in the meeting that would confirm or kill this hypothesis? If you can name that question, you are using the meeting to test your assumptions instead of defending them. Those confirm-or-kill questions also become some of your best questions in the needs analysis, two steps from now.

34.5 Now the first email: win the meeting, not the deal

With the people mapped, you write the first contact. The single most important idea is the job of that email: it is a door opener, not a proposal. Its only goal is to earn a reply and a short conversation. It is built directly on the value hypothesis from the research step, so it is relevant from the first line.

It is just as important to be clear about what the email is not. It is not a quote, it does not list specifications, and it does not ask for a decision. The substantiated value argument comes much later, after the conversation has confirmed the real need. Trying to close in the first email is the fastest way to get deleted.

A strong first email does three things and no more. Sales training calls this the opening statement, but the idea is plain, and it works for an email and a call alike:

  1. Purpose. Who you are and why you are reaching out now.
  2. Benefit. One pressure the person actually feels, drawn straight from the value hypothesis, connected to an outcome rather than to a product.
  3. Check. One small next step, a short call, not a full decision.

Most weak openings fail because they skip straight to a pitch before the person has accepted that the conversation is relevant. These three jobs force you to stay short, relevant, and easy to say yes to. The structure is role-neutral in shape but role-specific in content: the Benefit line is what changes from Lars to Sophie to Milan.

TipRole decides the tone, not personality
  • To Lars (decision-maker) — lead with uptime and rollout, the business outcome.
  • To Sophie (purchasing) — lead with cost logic and scope clarity.
  • To Milan (user) — lead with daily reliability on the floor.

That is all role information you already have from the map. What you deliberately do not do here is type the person, reading them as dominant or steady or anything else. That live reading comes in the next chapter, where DISC works from how the person actually replies. Today the email is purely role plus the value hypothesis, and that is enough to be relevant.

The First-Contact Gem (“First Contact (Kärcher Sales)”) drafts the email with Gemini right inside Gmail, so you are not copying text between tools. You give it the stakeholder map, the specific role you are writing to, and the value hypothesis, and ask for a short email built on Purpose, Benefit, and Check. If you leave out the role or the value hypothesis, it asks for them first instead of guessing.

  • Source — the stakeholder map, the role you are writing to, and the value hypothesis.
  • Output — a subject line plus one email under about 120 words, structured as Purpose / Benefit / Check.
  • Rule — one real pressure, no specifications, one clear call to action.

34.6 You keep the last word

Before anything reaches a real contact, run three quick tests on the draft:

  1. Does it name a real pressure, something this specific person actually feels, or has it drifted back into generic supplier language?
  2. Has it stayed a door opener, or has it crept toward a pitch with specs and a near-ask for a decision?
  3. The simplest test of all: would you, personally, be willing to sign it and send it?

If any answer is no, you revise before you send. The Gems remove the blank-page work and get you ninety percent of the way in seconds. The last ten percent, the judgment, is yours, and it is exactly where your value as a salesperson sits.

34.8 In summary

You now have the two things this step is built to deliver: a NorthStar stakeholder map with a goal, a pain, and an angle per role, and a role-fit first email to Lars that works toward the meeting rather than the deal, both built on the value hypothesis from research. That email opens the door.

The next chapter, Conversation Preparation, is about getting ready for the conversation itself: the mindset you walk in with, building rapport, and reading the room. That is where DISC comes in, reading the person live from how they actually replied, where Step 3 stayed purely role-based on purpose.

NoteWhat the research says

The chapter’s central claim, that you sell to people and not to a company, is one of the best-established ideas in B2B marketing. Webster and Wind (1972) modelled the organizational purchase as a group decision made in a Buying Center, with distinct roles, users, influencers, buyers, deciders, and gatekeepers, that each weigh the same offer differently. Fifty years on, Ehret, Johnston and Ritter (2024) confirm the buying center is still the signature concept of B2B, while it widens into networked “buying ecosystems”: more people, more outside influence, more digital touchpoints. The role model still holds; reality just got more crowded, which makes mapping the people before you write more important, not less (and the broader research field is mapped in the systematic review by Bilro et al. 2023).

That crowding is why a generic message fails. Consensus, not any single yes, is the real bottleneck: the research behind The Challenger Customer (Adamson et al. 2015) found that stakeholders often cannot even agree among themselves on what the problem is, and that the sellers who win equip an internal “mobilizer” to build that agreement. So the map is not academic, it is how you avoid writing to an average that persuades no one.

And a useful discipline-check: role is not the same as title. Bonoma’s classic “Major Sales: Who Really Does the Buying?” (1982/2006) showed that the org chart does not reveal who actually decides, influence and the real “power line” rarely match the formal hierarchy. That is exactly why every entry on your map stays a hypothesis to verify, never a fact to act on blindly.

When you do reach out, lead with value, not product. Terho, Haas, Eggert and Ulaga (2012) conceptualize value-based selling as orienting the whole interaction around the customer’s business outcome rather than product features, which is precisely what the Benefit line of the first email does, and why it is built on the value hypothesis rather than on a spec sheet.

NoteThe buying group is bigger than you think

Practitioner research puts hard numbers on the crowding. Gartner finds the typical B2B buying group involves roughly 6 to 10 decision-makers, working through four “buying jobs” (problem identification, solution exploration, requirements building, supplier selection); buying groups that reach consensus are about 2.5× more likely to land a high-quality, low-regret deal. The honest flip side: agreement is hard, and a large share of buying teams report real internal conflict during the decision. That is the reality your stakeholder map is built for.

A dose of realism on the email itself. Hard evidence on first-contact emails is mostly practitioner: analysing 20M+ sent messages, Woodpecker (2026) reports that short, well-targeted, genuinely personalized emails, with one or two follow-ups, earn far higher reply rates than generic blasts, though reply rates are modest in absolute terms even at their best. The lesson is not “email works magically”; it is that relevance beats volume, which is the entire point of mapping the people first. (Treat any specific figure as practitioner data to verify, not as a guarantee.)
