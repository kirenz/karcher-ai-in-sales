22  Needs Analysis

Walk into the conversation with a sharp, ordered set of questions and walk out with a confirmed need. AI drafts and sorts the questions and tags the answers; you have the conversation and decide what matters.

On the map above, the customer has moved into Evaluation: the question is no longer whether to act, but “what exactly do we need?”.

This is Step 5 of the sales cycle, and it is the hinge of the whole thing. Up to now you have built a value hypothesis in the research step, reached the right people in the contacts step, and prepared the human side of the meeting in conversation preparation. Now you are inside the meeting itself, at the needs analysis. The goal is not a method to memorize. The goal is a short, sharp set of questions going in, and a confirmed need profile coming out, ready to turn into a value argument in the next step. Questions in, need profile out.

  • Ask before you pitch: stay in discovery until the need is clear. Questions in, a confirmed need profile out.
  • Run the discovery order Situation → Need → Impact → Pay-off so each question is sharper and the customer names the business consequence in their own words.
  • Capture the whole need against six fixed dimensions (Service, Performance, Economy, Image, Comfort, Safety) — the empty boxes are your follow-up list, never a box to fill with a guess.
  • AI proposes, you decide: the Gem drafts the questions and sorts the answers, but you choose what to ask and confirm what you actually heard.

22.1 Ask before you pitch

Many conversations lose quality the moment the product comes too early. The seller moves quickly toward the solution, the customer’s need stays half-defined, and the benefit language that follows sounds generic, because it is not yet anchored in anything the customer has actually said. The problem is rarely the feature set. It is the weak bridge between the customer’s reality and the solution story.

The fix is simple in principle and hard in practice: ask first, argue later. Better questions at the start are exactly what make the value story at the end specific and convincing. So you deliberately slow down in the right place, stay in discovery until the need is clear, and only then move toward the solution. Two quiet tools make that happen. One gives you a question order that makes each question sharper. The other gives you a checklist of need dimensions so nothing important slips through. You never name either of them in front of the customer. Together they help you ask well and listen completely.

22.2 Two threads you pick up here

You do not start cold. From Step 2 you carry the value hypothesis — why Kärcher might matter to NorthStar (the downtime pressure, the fragmented fleet, the wish to standardize) — and that is exactly what you came to test. From Step 4 you carry the DISC read: how Lars and the others like to be engaged, which steers your pace, detail, and tone in the moment. What you hand on from here is no longer a guess, but a confirmed need and a clear profile.

22.3 A simple order makes questions sharper

A strong discovery sequence moves through four steps, in order — SNIP (Situation, Need, Impact, Pay-off), where the Need step is the gap: what is not working well enough. You do not drill the acronym; you keep the discipline of the order:

Step What it does The question gathers
Situation Understand the current setup, environment, and routines facts
Need Identify what is not working well enough, and what the customer wants to change tension
Impact Make the consequence of leaving that need unresolved visible a concrete, often quantifiable consequence
Pay-off Let the customer describe the better future they would actually value the future the customer wants

First you understand the situation, then surface the need, and only then develop it: the impact if nothing changes, and the pay-off if it does. Impact and Pay-off are the two steps most sellers underuse, even though they create the strongest movement — they turn a vague problem into customer-owned urgency and value, so do not jump to the pay-off before the customer has named the impact. That order is also what makes each question sharper: because each step builds on the last, the customer ends up describing the business consequence in their own words. The later value argument then rests on the customer’s own logic, not just your wording.

22.4 High-gain questions by role

A high-gain question does more than collect information. It makes the customer think. In practice, high-gain questions share three traits: they invite explanation instead of a yes-or-no answer, they connect a local issue to a broader business consequence, and they help the customer think forward, not only backward.

The four-step order stays fixed across the Buying Center; only the emphasis and the language change by role. Preparing both variants in advance is what lets you adapt to whoever is in the room without improvising: the role decides the wording, the order stays the same.

Lars Meijer, Regional Operations Director — uptime and continuity. Lars is the operational sponsor, so his questions live at the level of uptime, continuity, and rollout, and each one is a direct test of the value hypothesis from Step 2.

Step Question for Lars
Situation How are cleaning standards managed across your sites right now?
Need Where do inconsistent routines create the most pressure?
Impact What does unplanned downtime cost you across a busy week?
Pay-off What would reliable continuity make possible at scale?

Milan Bakker, Site Supervisor — daily site routines. Milan judges whether the pilot feels practical under real operating pressure, so his questions stay close to the floor. Same order as Lars, but the language is operational, not strategic.

Step Question for Milan
Situation Walk me through a normal cleaning shift on this site.
Need Where does the current setup slow your team down?
Impact When a machine goes down mid-shift, what actually happens?
Pay-off What would make handovers and issue-handling easier day to day?

22.5 Let the DISC read steer the flow

You walk in with a prepared question set, but you do not read it like a script. This is where the DISC observation from Step 4 earns its place. The questions are prepared in advance; how you ask them adapts live. If Lars comes across as direct and results-driven, you keep it tight, lead with the cost and the outcome, and do not over-explain. If Milan is more steady and detail-oriented, you slow down, give him room, and stay concrete and practical. Same questions, different delivery.

TipThe funnel is a guide, not a script

DISC stays a working hypothesis you adjust as the conversation unfolds, and the human always leads. Ask the next question only when it fits the moment, not because it is next on the list, and adapt the moment the customer gives you new information. The AI prepared the questions; you read the room.

22.6 Six dimensions capture the whole need

The question order makes each question sharper. The six need dimensions make sure you capture the whole need. After a meeting, teams usually leave with many notes but no clean view of what the customer values most. A few issues sound strong, some comments feel important, several observations seem useful, but the picture is uneven. The fix is to read the notes against a fixed checklist of six need dimensions. The acronym behind them, SPEICS (Service, Performance, Economy, Image, Comfort, Safety), stays in the background; the checklist is what you use.

Dimension In plain words
Service reliability, support, responsiveness, and continuity
Performance output quality, productivity, and result
Economy cost logic, business value, and commercial viability
Image reputation and outward perception
Comfort usability, routine, and ease in daily operation
Safety operational risk, continuity, and compliance-related security

The first three usually carry the commercial case and are easy to spot. The second three matter just as much but are easier to miss when notes are messy. The value of a fixed checklist is not only completeness, it is discipline: instead of assuming that one visible issue tells the whole story, you test the account against all six dimensions and keep only what the evidence actually supports.

22.6.1 No single voice covers the whole account

A complete map is not a box-ticking exercise. It is the safeguard against building a one-sided profile, because no single contact speaks for the whole account. In the NorthStar Buying Center, each stakeholder weights the same six dimensions differently:

  • Lars Meijer (Regional Operations Director) leads with performance and reliability — proof the pilot reduces disruption across sites.
  • Sophie Verbeek (Procurement) tests economy and value logic — whether the service component is justified and defendable internally.
  • Milan Bakker (Site Supervisor) judges comfort and daily usability — if onboarding and routines feel impractical, his confidence drops fast.
  • Eva van Dijk (HSE and Service Lead) needs safety and a clear support path before she will back a rollout.

If you only listen to Lars, you hear performance and reliability and you build a profile that looks finished but is one-sided, and it will fail at the next stakeholder. The checklist forces the useful question: whose need is still missing?

22.6.2 The empty boxes are the point

This is the core discipline of the chapter. The point of a six-point check is not to tick every box. It is to make the empty boxes visible. If a need has thin or no evidence, that is not a weakness in the customer, it is a missing question on your side. In the NorthStar case the strongest signals after a discovery visit tend to sit in Service, Performance, and Economy, while Comfort (Milan), Safety (Eva), and Image (NorthStar is itself a facility-services provider, so its cleaning quality shapes how its own customers judge it) are easy to underplay. The wrong response to a thin dimension is to invent an assumption so the profile looks complete. The right response is to mark it underexplored and prepare exactly one good follow-up, addressed to the person who owns that need:

  • Comfort gap → ask Milan about operator onboarding and routine simplification across sites.
  • Safety gap → ask Eva about the escalation path and support response when a unit fails.
  • Image gap → ask Lars whether service consistency affects how NorthStar’s own customers judge them.

One good question per gap is enough. An honest gap is more valuable than a confident guess. If a dimension genuinely is not commercially relevant at this stage, the right call may be not to force a question at all. The checklist sharpens judgment; it does not replace it.

22.7 The Needs-Analysis Gem builds your question set

You do not type any of this from a blank page. The Needs-Analysis Gem (“Needs-Analysis (Kärcher Sales)”) is a prepared expert that already knows the question order and the six need dimensions. It works in Google Docs, so the output is a living conversation guide you can edit. You attach the NorthStar briefing Doc and the Buying-Center Map Doc, and it does two jobs:

  • Drafts and sorts a question set in the Situation → Need → Impact → Pay-off order, with at least one high-gain question per role, deliberately aligned to the value hypothesis from Step 2, so the conversation tests what you actually believe.
  • Tags answers into the six need dimensions after the conversation, quotes the evidence, rates each as strong, weak, or underexplored, and flags what is still thin.

You normally just open the prepared Gem and attach your input — the briefing and the map Docs to draft questions, your visit-notes Doc to tag them.

Prompts you can copy and try. You supply the roles you are meeting and, when you have them, the briefing and Buying-Center Map Docs (before the meeting) or your visit-notes Doc (after it). The questions, and the need profile, are what the Gem produces — never describe the needs yourself.

The Gem is genuinely useful: it drafts more questions than you need, which is good, and it sorts messy notes in seconds. But you stay in charge on both sides. On the draft, cut the questions that sound forced or that quietly presuppose your solution, and keep the strongest one or two per step that you would actually be comfortable asking. On the tagging, the model overclassifies — it reads ambition into a passing comment and marks a need as strong when the note barely supports it. So make two passes: first confirm every tag is backed by something the customer actually said and remove the confident guesses; then look at what it flags as empty, because that is your follow-up list. The model proposes; you decide what to ask, and you decide what you really heard, and you verify it before it shapes the conversation, in line with the Module 0 quality gate.

22.8 You walk out with a confirmed need

This is the moment the whole thread has been building toward. Back in Step 2, the need and the BANT picture (Budget, Authority, Need, Timing) were a reasoned hypothesis; here, in the conversation, that guess becomes ground truth.

Was a hypothesis (Step 2) Now confirmed (Step 5)
Likely need: uptime, standardization A real need, in the customer’s own words
BANT: budget, authority, timing assumed BANT checked against what the customer actually said

You leave with two artifacts. The first is the conversation guide, the ordered set of questions you took into the meeting. The second, and the one that steers everything after it, is the need profile: the answers tagged into the six dimensions, with the strong needs confirmed and the thin ones named as gaps. That profile is the filter for the next step. In Value Argumentation you only build arguments that map to a confirmed need, and you prioritize them by the dimensions the customer weighted most heavily. A complete, honest profile is what makes the value story specific; a thin one makes every benefit sound generic and easy to match. Better questions beat better pitches: AI drafts and sorts the questions and tags the answers, but it cannot have the conversation for you, and it cannot decide what matters.

22.10 In summary

Needs analysis is compact, but used with discipline it lifts the quality of everything downstream. Ask before you pitch. The Situation → Need → Impact → Pay-off order makes each question sharper and moves the customer from describing facts to naming consequences in their own words. The six dimensions — Service, Performance, Economy, Image, Comfort, and Safety — make sure you capture the whole need and surface the boxes you did not yet hear. Role variants for Lars and Milan adjust the language while the order stays stable, and the DISC read from Step 4 steers the pace, the detail, and the tone live. The Needs-Analysis Gem drafts and sorts the question set and tags the answers, but you decide what to ask and own what you heard.

You hand on a confirmed need, not a guess. The next chapter, Value Argumentation, turns it into a customer-specific value argument — specific precisely because the need underneath it is real.

NoteWhat the research says

The chapter’s central move — ask before you pitch — is one of the best-established findings in selling research. Saxe and Weitz (1982) defined and measured customer orientation (the SOCO scale): the degree to which a salesperson works to help the customer make a good buying decision rather than push for a quick close. Customer-oriented selling is tied to the seller’s actual ability to help and to the quality of the relationship. And it is not just a “nice” posture: Terho, Eggert, Haas and Ulaga (2015) show empirically that sales strategy translates into performance through customer orientation and value-based selling. The need you genuinely understand is the mechanism that turns effort into results.

Why the Situation → Need → Impact → Pay-off order works is the most-studied question in discovery. Rackham’s SPIN Selling (1988), built on Huthwaite’s analysis of tens of thousands of real sales calls, distinguishes four question types — Situation, Problem, Implication, and Need-payoff — and finds that in larger, complex sales it is the Implication and Need-payoff questions (our Impact and Pay-off) that correlate with success, while situation and problem questions alone do not. Implication questions make the consequence of a problem felt; need-payoff questions get the customer to state the value of a solution in their own words. That is exactly why the order is doing double duty: it sharpens each question, and it moves persuasion from your assertion to the customer’s own logic. (Honest scope: the effect is strongest in complex sales; in small transactional deals it matters less.)

Capturing the whole need is a listening problem, and listening is measurable. Comer and Drollinger (1999) framed active empathetic listening as a driver of selling success, trust, and relationship quality; Drollinger, Comer and Warrington (2006) validated it as a scale with three parts — sensing (hearing the unsaid), processing (remembering and connecting), and responding (visibly reacting and probing). That is the human skill behind the six-dimension check: the order gets the customer talking, and disciplined listening is what makes sure you heard all of it, not just the loud parts.

Finally, adapting the questions by role without changing the logic is adaptive selling, the same construct that anchors Step 4 (Weitz, Sujan & Sujan 1986; Spiro & Weitz 1990). The discovery-specific point is narrow: the role decides the language of a question, never the order, and the live DISC read decides the delivery. Your prepared variants for Lars and Milan are that adaptation done on purpose rather than improvised.

NoteWhat discovery calls actually look like

Conversation-intelligence data puts numbers on the craft. Analysing large samples of recorded discovery calls, Gong reports that successful calls tend to ask a moderate number of questions (roughly 11–14 — enough to go deep, not so many it feels like an interrogation), spread them across the call, use follow-up questions that build directly on the buyer’s last answer, and keep a talk-to-listen ratio that favours listening (the seller talking well under half the time). Treat these as practitioner benchmarks to verify, not laws — but they line up neatly with the academic story: fewer, sharper questions and more listening.
