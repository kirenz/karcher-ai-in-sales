38  Presentation & Demo

Turn the confirmed value argument from Step 6 into a short, role-aware customer presentation and a storyboard for the real product demonstration: the slides set up the argument, the demonstration earns the trust, and both pull the same need-and-value thread so they tell one coherent story.

On the map above, the customer reaches Decision and asks “does this actually do the job?”. Your slides set up the case, and a live demonstration lets them see the answer.

This is Step 7 of the sales cycle, and it is the most practical step — the one where everything finally faces the customer. Up to now the work has been preparation: research, first contact, conversation prep, discovery, and value argumentation. You walked out of the value-argument step with one artifact that steers this whole chapter: a customer-specific value building block for NorthStar — the feature (three Kärcher BR 45/22 C Bp Pack scrubber driers plus onboarding and a service-start package), the advantage, the benefit tied to confirmed needs, prioritised by what the customer weighted. That block is the input here. Out of it come two outputs: a lean presentation that carries the value story and points to a decision, and a plan for the real demonstration that proves the story is true. From here the thread hands on to closing.

  • Turn the confirmed value block into a lean three-slide presentation (situation / fit / decision), one idea per slide, plus a storyboard for the real demonstration.
  • The slides set up the argument; the demo earns the trust — both pull the same need-and-value thread, so they tell one coherent story.
  • Reframe one stable commercial core by role (operations vs procurement); never split the account into three different offers.
  • Show the representative hardest case, not a staged one, and verify every figure — the real deliverable is the coherence between slides and demo.

38.1 A presentation is not a brochure

A customer presentation is not a brochure. The goal is not to show everything you know about the product range; it is to help the Buying Center understand the decision in front of them and see the next step clearly. Presentation software, and now AI, makes it very easy to produce volume. The discipline here is the opposite: reduce, sequence, and point to a decision.

Three slides force that simplicity. Each slide has exactly one job:

  • Slide 1 — the situation and why now. The current pressure, the risk, the reason action matters now.
  • Slide 2 — why the offer fits. The solution linked back to the customer’s actual priorities, with the proof in place.
  • Slide 3 — the decision and the next step. Concrete, with one owner and one date, not a vague summary.

If a slide is doing two jobs, split it or cut it. The customer does not need a twenty-slide walk-through to decide whether a pilot makes sense; they need clarity on the problem, the logic of the recommendation, and the next action that follows.

TipThe worked NorthStar pitch

Three BR 45/22 C Bp Pack scrubber driers in the pilot, each slide doing one job, every claim tracing back to the value block:

  • Why now. A mixed, aging fleet across sites, machines that break down mid-shift, a push to standardize for uptime and service continuity — the operational pressure NorthStar already feels.
  • Why this fits. Three scrubber driers plus onboarding and a service-start package, so the ramp-up is fast and low-disruption. The offer answers the pressure named on the first slide.
  • The decision. Run the pilot, measure what matters (uptime and service response), set a clear review point. Owner: Lars. Review in six weeks.

That is a decision story, not a brochure — and it is exactly what the demo will then prove.

38.2 One core story, reframed by role

The Buying Center does not say yes for one reason, so a single emphasis rarely lands with everyone. You have met this twice already: it is the adaptive selling you practised from conversation preparation on, and you already built the two role variants in the value argument — Lars leading on uptime, Sophie on total cost of ownership. The only new question here is what happens to that one story when it becomes a deck: what must stay fixed, and what may move?

The commercial core stays fixed — the pilot, the three units, the service logic, and the single next step are constant. Only the framing moves: the headline, the order of the proof points, and which economy view leads (operational for Lars, total cost of ownership for Sophie). What you never do is split the account into three disconnected decks, which make it feel sold three different things. So when you reframe, say explicitly what stays and what changes, and ask for one line confirming the core is unchanged — that is what keeps the two versions coherent for the same account.

One word on the economy view, because on a slide it is where trust is won or lost. Lead it as total cost of ownership rather than unit price — you built that bridge in Step 6. The discipline that is specific to the presentation is restraint: a chart that looks overly precise without the evidence behind it makes the customer doubt the whole argument. Quantify only what you can defend, keep the assumptions visible, and show directional operating logic rather than pretending the ROI is exact to the last decimal.

38.3 Slides set up the argument, the demo earns the trust

It helps to be honest about what a presentation can and cannot do. A good, short, role-aware presentation gets you the conversation and frames the decision. But in this industry the decision often turns on the demonstration. A site supervisor who watches a scrubber drier leave a floor clean and dry in a single pass in their own cleaning window believes it in a way no slide can match. So treat the presentation and the demo as two halves of one move: the slides set up the argument, and the demonstration earns the trust. That is why the demo is planned with the same care as the slides, and from the same thread.

A demonstration is a sequence of small proofs, not a feature tour. The storyboard has two parts, and both matter. The ordered sequence is the dramaturgy: for each step you set out what you show on the machine, the one line you say while you show it, and the objection most likely to come up there with your answer ready. The talk track is short on purpose — the machine is the argument, you are the guide. The setup checklist is the unglamorous half that decides whether the demo works at all: the kit and machine, the fresh-water fill and a charged battery, the floor conditions on site, and who does what on the day. A great storyboard that fails because the battery was flat still loses the room.

TipThe worked NorthStar storyboard

Each proof tied to a confirmed need, each with its objection anticipated — the demo telling the same story as the slides:

  • Uptime. Show that one fully charged battery runs a full cleaning window in Eco!Mode, because mid-shift breakdowns are the pain they named. Objection to pre-empt: does the battery last a shift?
  • Cleaning result. Run a single scrubbing pass on a floor that resembles their hardest real case, including edges and a structured surface where the roller brush and KART head earn their keep, and let the result speak before you do. Objection to pre-empt: does it handle our floor types?
  • Service continuity. Walk through the service-start package, and answer the question every operator has — who responds when it breaks down. Objection to pre-empt: service response.

If a feature does not answer a need on the list, it does not go in the storyboard.

38.4 One thread, one coherent story

The reason you build the presentation and the demo from the same value building block is coherence. When the slide says the pilot protects uptime and the demonstration then shows the machine ready in seconds, the customer experiences one argument from two directions, and trust compounds. When slides and demo contradict each other, even slightly, the whole case wobbles.

So before you present, lay the two outputs side by side and check that every claim on a slide has a matching proof in the storyboard, and that every demo step earns a point the slides actually make. One thread, one story, two surfaces. That alignment is the real deliverable of this step — not the slides or the storyboard on their own.

38.5 The two Gems draft the slides and the demo

You do not start from a blank canvas, and you do not start from a generic template — you start from the value you already confirmed. Two prepared experts cover this step, and as always with anything customer-facing, the Gem drafts and you decide.

ImportantThe Gem drafts the content, not the customer deck

What the Presentation Gem returns is a content draft: the structure, the proof points, and the wording for each slide. Use it as inspiration for how to prepare the argument, not as a finished presentation. Nothing the Gem or Canvas produces is shown or sent to a customer as-is.

Every customer-facing presentation is built in the official Kärcher Google Slides template and follows the Kärcher corporate identity — its layouts, fonts, colours, and logo. So the move is always two steps: let the Gem shape the content, then transfer that content into the Kärcher CI template, and present that version. The same holds for any customer-facing slides or leave-behind: AI helps you think and draft, the brand-facing artifact is built in Kärcher’s approved template.

38.5.2 The Demo Gem plans the real demonstration

The second is the Demo Gem (Demo Storyboard (Kärcher Sales)). It runs in Google Docs and starts from the top needs you confirmed in the needs analysis. From those it builds the storyboard: what to show on the machine, in which order, with a short talk-track line and an anticipated objection and answer for each step, plus a setup checklist. It pulls the same need-and-value thread as the presentation, so the demo proves exactly the points the slides claim, and product content comes from the same YelloFile you used to build the value argument in Step 6, so you are working from approved material, not improvising.

Prompts you can copy and try. You attach two things — the value block Doc (which carries the confirmed needs) and the product’s YelloFile (the approved product facts) — and the ordered storyboard, the talk track, the objections, and the setup checklist are what the Gem produces. You never script the demo yourself. The standard move is to attach both and ask for the storyboard plus the setup checklist. This is the example prompt the Gem itself suggests:

38.5.3 The quality gate before customer use

This is the Step-0 quality gate (Prompt, Validate) applied to a presentation and a demo. Before anything goes to the customer, run three checks:

  1. One idea per slide. Does each slide carry a single idea, is the proof present, is the value explicit, and is the next step with an owner and a date clear?
  2. Figures verified. Quantify only what you can defend, keep assumptions visible, and verify every figure — a chart that looks precise without evidence makes the customer doubt the whole argument.
  3. Every demo step tied to a confirmed need. Is each step anchored to a need the customer named, and is the kit confirmed against the site?

Presentation software makes weak logic look polished, and AI makes that happen even faster, so the final discipline is not creation, it is control. Two rules close this step. The customer-facing deck is always rebuilt in the official Kärcher Google Slides template (Kärcher CI) — the Gem draft is inspiration for the content, never the deck that goes out. And the Step-0 approval route still applies: formal proposals and total-cost content need a sign-off, not just a rep send.

38.7 In summary

This step turns the confirmed value argument into a decision basis for the Buying Center. A lean three-slide presentation — situation, fit, decision — does one job per slide, because working memory is limited and a slide you read aloud competes with you (Mayer & Moreno 2003; Kalyuga, Chandler & Sweller 1999). One stable commercial core is reframed by role, operations for Lars and total cost of ownership for Sophie, without splitting the account. The real demonstration then proves what the slides claim, because direct, unambiguous product experience convinces in a way a slide cannot (Hoch & Deighton 1989; Hoch & Ha 1986) — provided the case shown is representative, not staged (Hoch 2002). Both outputs are drawn from the same value building block, so they cannot contradict each other, and the Presentation Gem and Demo Gem draft both in minutes while you own the story, the proof, and the next step.

The output is a lean, role-aware presentation and a demonstration plan that tell one coherent story, ready for the Buying Center. That decision basis is the bridge into the closing step, where — after the proposal and the demo — you make the next step concrete with closing wording, answers to the typical objections (including Sophie’s price objection, deliberately held until there), and a way to rehearse the close before you make it. The same NorthStar story continues, and so does the thread.

NoteWhat the research says

The two halves of this step rest on two well-established literatures, and it is worth being precise about what each one shows — and where it stops.

Why the demonstration convinces. The strongest evidence for “the demo earns the trust” comes from the research on learning from direct product experience. Hoch and Deighton (1989) model how people learn from using a product and show that what is learned from direct experience tends to be vivid, held with confidence, and hard to revise — an experienced result writes itself in differently than a described claim. Hoch and Ha (1986) add the decisive condition: when the product experience is unambiguous, it dominates judgement and overrides advertising or sales claims; when it is ambiguous, the claims fill the gap and can even colour what the customer thinks they saw. The practical reading is exact: aim the demo at a clear result on a case that resembles the customer’s hardest real situation, and let the result speak before you do. A vague demonstration throws away the very advantage that makes it stronger than a slide.

Why the lean slide works. “One idea per slide” is not an aesthetic preference; it follows from how working memory is loaded. Mayer and Moreno (2003) summarise the multimedia-learning principles around a hard limit on working-memory capacity and give concrete levers to reduce extraneous load — cut material that does not serve the point (coherence), highlight the few things that matter (signaling), and avoid making two channels compete. Kalyuga, Chandler and Sweller (1999) sharpen one lever into a rule: the redundancy effect — identical text on a slide that the presenter reads aloud at the same time tends to hinder learning rather than help it, because the audience burns capacity reconciling the two sources. That is the evidence behind the hardest practical rule in this chapter: do not read a full slide. The slide carries one idea; your voice carries the rest. (These are learning theories, not sales-outcome studies — they explain the mechanism behind the slide and demo disciplines; the sales-context grounding, that a presentation should lead with the few points that matter and move to a decision, comes from resonating focus and the narrative thread of the pitch.)

ImportantThe demo can mislead, too

The honest counter-evidence sits inside the same literature. Hoch (2002) argues that product experience is seductive: precisely because an experienced result feels self-evident, it can mislead when the case shown is not representative. A rigged or flattering demonstration wins the moment and loses the trust. So the discipline that travels with the demo’s power is not optional — show the representative hardest case, not a staged showcase, and treat every figure on the economy slide as a draft until it is verified. The demonstration’s persuasive force is a responsibility, not a trick.

NoteWhy the few live moments have to land

Gartner’s research on the B2B buying journey finds that buyers spend only around 17% of their total purchase time meeting with potential suppliers — and when several suppliers are in play, any one sales rep may get just 5–6%. Most of the journey now happens without a rep in the room. The implication for this step is direct: the few live moments you do get, the presentation and above all the demonstration, have to do disproportionate work. That is the case for preparing both with this much care — they may be the only chance to turn a value argument into a decision.
