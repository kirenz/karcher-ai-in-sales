38  Presentation & Demo

Turn the confirmed value argument from Step 6 into a storyboard for the real product demonstration — a sequence of proofs, each tied to a confirmed need, that shows the customer on the machine what a slide can only claim.

On the map above, the customer reaches Decision and asks “does this actually do the job?”. A live demonstration lets them see the answer.

This is Step 7 of the sales cycle, and it is the most practical step — the one where everything finally faces the customer. 38.1 Slides set up the argument, the demo earns the trust

It helps to be honest about what a presentation can and cannot do. A good, short, role-aware presentation gets you the conversation and frames the decision. But in this industry the decision often turns on the demonstration. A site supervisor who watches a scrubber drier leave a floor clean and dry in a single pass in their own cleaning window believes it in a way no slide can match. So treat the presentation and the demo as two halves of one move: the slides set up the argument, and the demonstration earns the trust. That is why the demo is planned with the same care as the slides, and from the same thread.

A demonstration is a sequence of small proofs, not a feature tour. The storyboard has two parts, and both matter. The ordered sequence is the dramaturgy: for each step you set out what you show on the machine, the one line you say while you show it, and the objection most likely to come up there with your answer ready. The talk track is short on purpose — the machine is the argument, you are the guide. The setup checklist is the unglamorous half that decides whether the demo works at all: the kit and machine, the fresh-water fill and a charged battery, the floor conditions on site, and who does what on the day. A great storyboard that fails because the battery was flat still loses the room.

TipThe worked NorthStar storyboard

Each proof tied to a confirmed need, each with its objection anticipated — the demo telling the same story as the slides:

  • Uptime. Show that one fully charged battery runs a full cleaning window in Eco!Mode, because mid-shift breakdowns are the pain they named. Objection to pre-empt: does the battery last a shift?
  • Cleaning result. Run a single scrubbing pass on a floor that resembles their hardest real case, including edges and a structured surface where the roller brush and KART head earn their keep, and let the result speak before you do. Objection to pre-empt: does it handle our floor types?
  • Service continuity. Walk through the service-start package, and answer the question every operator has — who responds when it breaks down. Objection to pre-empt: service response.

If a feature does not answer a need on the list, it does not go in the storyboard.

38.2 One thread, one coherent story

The reason you build the presentation and the demo from the same value building block is coherence. When the slide says the pilot protects uptime and the demonstration then shows the machine ready in seconds, the customer experiences one argument from two directions, and trust compounds. When slides and demo contradict each other, even slightly, the whole case wobbles.

So before you present, lay the two outputs side by side and check that every claim on a slide has a matching proof in the storyboard, and that every demo step earns a point the slides actually make. One thread, one story, two surfaces. That alignment is the real deliverable of this step — not the slides or the storyboard on their own.

38.3 The Demo Gem plans the real demonstration

You do not start from a blank page — you start from the value you already confirmed. One prepared expert covers this step, and as always with anything customer-facing, the Gem drafts and you decide.

The Demo Gem (Demo Storyboard (Kärcher Sales)) runs in Google Docs and starts from the top needs you confirmed in the needs analysis. From those it builds the storyboard: what to show on the machine, in which order, with a short talk-track line and an anticipated objection and answer for each step, plus a setup checklist. It pulls the same need-and-value thread as the presentation, so the demo proves exactly the points the slides claim, and product content comes from the same YellowFile you used to build the value argument in Step 6, so you are working from approved material, not improvising.

Prompts you can copy and try. You attach two things — the value block Doc (which carries the confirmed needs) and the product’s YellowFile (the approved product facts) — and the ordered storyboard, the talk track, the objections, and the setup checklist are what the Gem produces. You never script the demo yourself. The standard move is to attach both and ask for the storyboard plus the setup checklist. This is the example prompt the Gem itself suggests:

You can also make the need-tie the explicit spine (each proof anchored to one named need), or ask for a short two-or-three-proof version for a tight slot.

38.3.1 The quality gate before customer use

This is the Step-0 quality gate (Prompt, Validate) applied to the demonstration. Before the storyboard steers a customer meeting, run three checks:

  1. Every demo step tied to a confirmed need. Is each step anchored to a need the customer named, and does anything that answers no need stay out?
  2. The case is representative. Does the demo show the customer’s hardest real situation, not a staged showcase — and is the kit confirmed against the site?
  3. Figures verified. Quantify only what you can defend, keep assumptions visible, and verify every figure before it is said out loud or lands on a slide.

AI makes a polished-sounding plan fast, so the final discipline is not creation, it is control. Two rules close this step. The customer-facing presentation is always built in the official Kärcher Google Slides template (Kärcher CI) — AI drafts are never the deck that goes out. And the Step-0 approval route still applies: formal proposals and total-cost content need a sign-off, not just a rep send.

B · Do it for real: your own account

3. Pick a real account, attach its value-block Doc (and the YellowFile for the machine), and watch the demo plan come together in minutes — then run the same coherence check against your presentation.

As you debrief, ask the questions that matter:

  • Which demo step rests on the least representative case, and how would you make the proof unambiguous?
  • Where is a figure still unverified, and which one would you check first?
  • Which planned step answers no confirmed need — and what does the storyboard gain when it goes?

38.5 In summary

This step turns the confirmed value argument into a decision basis for the Buying Center. The presentation — built in the official Kärcher Google Slides template, lean, and reframed for the role you are meeting — sets up the case. The real demonstration then proves what the slides claim, because direct, unambiguous product experience convinces in a way a slide cannot (Hoch & Deighton 1989; Hoch & Ha 1986) — provided the case shown is representative, not staged (Hoch 2002). Demo and deck are drawn from the same value building block, so they cannot contradict each other, and the Demo Gem drafts the storyboard in minutes while you own the story, the proof, and the next step.

The output is a demonstration plan that tells one coherent story with your presentation, ready for the Buying Center. That decision basis is the bridge into the closing step, where — after the proposal and the demo — you make the next step concrete with closing wording, answers to the typical objections (including Sophie’s price objection, deliberately held until there), and a way to rehearse the close before you make it. The same NorthStar story continues, and so does the thread.

NoteWhat the research says

The heart of this step rests on a well-established literature on learning from product experience, and it is worth being precise about what it shows — and where it stops.

Why the demonstration convinces. The strongest evidence for “the demo earns the trust” comes from the research on learning from direct product experience. Hoch and Deighton (1989) model how people learn from using a product and show that what is learned from direct experience tends to be vivid, held with confidence, and hard to revise — an experienced result writes itself in differently than a described claim. Hoch and Ha (1986) add the decisive condition: when the product experience is unambiguous, it dominates judgement and overrides advertising or sales claims; when it is ambiguous, the claims fill the gap and can even colour what the customer thinks they saw. The practical reading is exact: aim the demo at a clear result on a case that resembles the customer’s hardest real situation, and let the result speak before you do. A vague demonstration throws away the very advantage that makes it stronger than a slide.

ImportantThe demo can mislead, too

The honest counter-evidence sits inside the same literature. Hoch (2002) argues that product experience is seductive: precisely because an experienced result feels self-evident, it can mislead when the case shown is not representative. A rigged or flattering demonstration wins the moment and loses the trust. So the discipline that travels with the demo’s power is not optional — show the representative hardest case, not a staged showcase, and treat every figure on the economy slide as a draft until it is verified. The demonstration’s persuasive force is a responsibility, not a trick.

NoteWhy the few live moments have to land

Gartner’s research on the B2B buying journey finds that buyers spend only around 17% of their total purchase time meeting with potential suppliers — and when several suppliers are in play, any one sales rep may get just 5–6%. Most of the journey now happens without a rep in the room. The implication for this step is direct: the few live moments you do get, the presentation and above all the demonstration, have to do disproportionate work. That is the case for preparing both with this much care — they may be the only chance to turn a value argument into a decision.
