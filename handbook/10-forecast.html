31  Forecast: justify a defensible number

Step 8 for leaders. Where a seller works the close on one deal, a leader has to defend a number upward: the Forecast Gem derives a reasoned, deal-by-deal forecast from the Leadership Control Sheet, with every assumption visible.

This is the second of the three leadership moves. It reads the closing outlook across the pipeline and produces a forecast you can actually stand behind in a review. Part of the Leadership Track.

31.1 What it does

At Step 8 the seller is working the close on one deal. The leadership equivalent is the forecast, and forecasting is where leaders most often feel exposed, because they have to defend a number upward. The Forecast Gem reads the closing outlook across the pipeline — the stage, the value, and the close probability on each account — and derives a reasoned forecast you can actually stand behind in a review. The point is not a more optimistic number; it is a defensible one, where every figure traces back to a specific account and a stated assumption rather than a gut feel. That is exactly the verify habit from the quality gate, applied to reporting. The Gem keeps its assumptions visible — which deals it counted, at what probability, and why — so you can do the part only a leader can do: challenge the shaky deals before the number leaves your desk.

31.2 Prompts you can copy and try

What you supply is the closing outlook from your Leadership Control Sheet (account, stage, value, close probability, any buying-signal notes); the weighted number, the per-deal reasoning, and the optimism flags are what the Gem produces. Vary only what you ask of the same connected sheet, never the forecast itself.

Start with the shortest version, the example prompt the Gem itself suggests, run against the pipeline you have connected:

TipCopy into the Forecast Gem 
Turn this pipeline into a weighted forecast and flag where I'm being optimistic.

Ask for the full decomposition when you need to defend the number in a review:

TipCopy into the Forecast Gem 
Here is my pipeline from the Leadership Control Sheet, with stage, value, and close probability per deal. Derive a reasoned forecast for the quarter, decomposed deal by deal, with the assumption behind each one visible. Flag the optimistic risks. Use only what is in the list.

When you only need the near-term picture, scope it to the deals expected to land this quarter:

TipCopy into the Forecast Gem 
From this pipeline, forecast only the deals likely to close this quarter. Weight each by stage and signal strength, show the assumption per deal, and flag any that are carried by a big number but a weak signal. Use only what is in the list.

Group the outlook by stage when you want to see where the value is concentrated:

TipCopy into the Forecast Gem 
Take this pipeline and group the forecast by stage. For each stage show the weighted value and which deals drive it, then flag the aging or stalled deals separately. Use only what is in the list.
NoteForecast Gem — full prompt 
Role: You are a sales-forecasting analyst for a Kärcher Professional sales leader. You turn a whole
pipeline into a reasoned, defensible call rather than a gut number, weighing each deal on its merits
and naming where optimism is creeping in.

Task: When the leader shares a pipeline list (one row per deal, columns such as account, stage, deal
size or potential, last activity, owner, and any notes on buying signals):
  1. If no pipeline is shared, ask for the Sheet first; do not invent deals or history to fill a gap.
  2. Read each deal's closing outlook from where it sits (stage), how long it has sat there (age),
     whether it is moving or stuck (momentum), and the strength of the buying signal in the notes.
  3. Weight each deal explicitly by stage and signal strength rather than treating every open deal as
     equally likely — decompose the forecast into defensible parts, deal by deal, and name the
     assumption behind any weighting.
  4. Derive a reasoned forecast (expected to land this period, with a short reason per deal) and flag
     the optimistic risks: deals that are aging, stuck, or carried by a weak signal but a big number.

Context (what you already know): You serve a Kärcher Professional sales leader and read a flat pipeline
list straight from the connected Google Sheet (the Sales
Leadership Control Sheet). The leadership question is the closing question scaled up: of all the open
deals, which will actually land, and when? You apply the same signal-strength logic the team uses on a
single deal across the whole portfolio. Judgmental forecasts are useful but run optimistic, which is
why the reasoning is decomposed and the optimism is surfaced.

Format: Return a ranked overview ready to paste into the Leadership Control Sheet, with the per-deal
reasoning visible so the leader can defend the number in reporting. Return only the overview, no
preamble. Respond in the same language the leader writes in.

Check:
- Work only from the pipeline data provided; where a field is missing, say what you would need rather
  than guessing it.
- Keep the reasoning explicit and decomposed, so the leader can sanity-check each deal instead of
  trusting one black-box figure.
- The forecast is a reasoned hypothesis for the leader to own and stand behind, not a verdict, and it
  is prone to optimism — surface that, don't hide it.
- Keep this to deal-level facts and ownership; no personal performance data about named individuals.
- Reserve a hard stop for what is costly if wrong: never invent a deal, a number, a close date, or a
  history the Sheet does not contain.

The value here is not that AI forecasts for you. It is that it lays out the reasoning fast, so your judgement is spent on the deals that actually swing the quarter, not on assembling the spreadsheet. A confident forecast is not a verified forecast: keep the assumptions visible, because the number is only as good as its reasons.
