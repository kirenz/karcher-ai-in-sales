35  Conversation Preparation

Use AI to prepare the human side of the meeting, a fitting opening, what to listen for, and a first read of the person, then put the tool down and have the conversation yourself.

On the map above, the customer is still weighing options in Consideration. This is the last move before the meeting, where you prepare the human side of the conversation.

This is Step 4 of the sales cycle, and it is deliberately the shortest. You arrive with everything the earlier steps produced: the NorthStar account brief and the value hypothesis from research, the stakeholder map from the Buying Center step, and a first email already sent to Lars Meijer, who has now replied — turning a mapped role into a named person to prepare for, even if the last step started without a name.

The briefing is sharp, but it cannot do the two things the meeting depends on: getting Lars to trust you, and making you actually hear what he is worried about. AI cannot do those for you either. What it can do is get you ready. So the line that runs through this step is: AI sharpens your preparation, and the conversation stays human.

That makes this step different from the research-heavy ones before it. There is no large workflow here, just three things to prepare before you walk in, one framework to read the person live, and one Gem that gets you ready and then steps back.

  • The shortest step, on purpose: AI sharpens your preparation, the conversation stays human.
  • Prepare three things before you walk in — confidence, conviction, context. AI helps you prepare all three.
  • Use DISC as an observation framework to read the person live and adapt your opening, pace, and detail — the type is a hypothesis, read in the room, never AI-profiled from someone’s public footprint.
  • Prepared awareness, never scripted warmth: stop at the hand-off line, where the tool ends and you listen.

35.1 Good preparation changes the meeting before you speak

Rapport is often treated as something that simply happens in the room. In practice, the quality of that connection is shaped before the meeting even starts. Three things should be in place when you walk in, and weak meetings usually break in one of them.

  • Confidence is your own state: prepared, calm, and clear on the goal for the conversation.
  • Conviction is being able to say why the Kärcher solution and service fit NorthStar, not just recite specifications. For Lars, that means uptime across sites, service continuity, and a low-risk pilot on the three scrubber driers.
  • Context is knowing what actually matters to the person in front of you.

AI can help you prepare all three. It can pull Lars’s likely priorities and risks into one place, clarify the value logic so your conviction feels natural rather than rehearsed, and remind you what to listen for. What it cannot do is supply the confidence or the conviction itself.

35.2 What AI prepares, what stays human

The boundary is worth making concrete: everything else in this step lives on the AI side of it, and you have to cross back to the human side to use it.

AI can prepare Only you can do, live
A fitting opening for Lars Listen for what actually changes
A short list of what to listen for Adjust when priorities shift mid-conversation
His context and likely priorities Decide in the moment: probe, pause, or move on
ImportantPrepared awareness, never scripted warmth

This is the central guardrail of the chapter. Use AI for genuine awareness of Lars’s pressures, never to script warmth you do not feel. Prepared awareness means walking in with real understanding of his likely priorities, so your early questions are relevant. Scripted warmth is the opposite: AI-generated empathy lines, rehearsed curiosity, friendliness with no real listening behind it. Customers sense that very quickly, and it weakens trust instead of building it. The openers you prepare are starting points for a real conversation, not lines to perform.

35.3 DISC: a way to read the person live

The one new idea in this step is DISC, a simple and well-known model of four communication styles. We do not use it to put people in boxes. We use it as an observation framework: a way to read the person in front of you and adapt how you open, how fast you go, and how much detail you give.

The four types fall into two rough groups.

Type Recognize Adapt
Dominant brief, decisive, results-first lead with the outcome, stay concise
Influential warm, talkative, big-picture allow some rapport, paint the vision
Steady calm, careful, relationship-led slow down, reassure, take off the pressure
Conscientious precise, detailed, asks for proof bring facts, figures, and references

Dominant and Influential types tend to move faster; Steady and Conscientious types prefer a calmer pace and more detail. You get a first hint from how the person replied to your first email: a short, decisive reply points toward Dominant, a warm and expansive one toward Influential, a long and question-rich one toward Conscientious. If Sophie in procurement later replies in that careful, detail-seeking way, that would point toward the Conscientious end, a hypothesis you would still confirm in the room.

WarningTwo guardrails that make DISC safe to use
  1. The type is always a working hypothesis, never a fixed label. You keep checking and adjusting it as the conversation unfolds.
  2. DISC is read live, in the real conversation, not AI-profiled from someone’s public footprint. We never let a tool scan a person online and declare a personality type; that would be both unreliable and uncomfortable. The first read you prepare, including the one the Gem offers from the email reply, is only a starting point you confirm or correct in the first couple of minutes.

This read does real work later. It steers the flow of the needs analysis in the next step: the pace you set, the level of detail you bring, and the tone you take are all shaped by the type you are reading.

35.4 The Rapport Gem: prepare the human moment

The Gem for this step is the Rapport Gem (“Rapport (Kärcher Sales)”). You open it, attach the Buying Center Map Doc you built earlier, and for each key stakeholder it returns their likely communication style, one or two genuine openers, a short list of what to listen for, a first DISC type hypothesis read from how they replied to your first email, and a mindset reminder. It deliberately ends with a hand-off line, so the tool stops where the conversation begins.

  • Input — the Buying Center Map Doc from the contacts step, plus how each person replied to the first email.
  • Output — per stakeholder: likely style, one or two genuine openers, what to listen for, a first DISC hypothesis labelled as a hypothesis, and a mindset reminder.
  • Check — openers genuine rather than manipulative, the type kept as a hypothesis rather than a box, and the hand-off line left intact.

Prompts you can copy and try. You attach your Buying Center Map Doc and describe how each person replied to your first email; you never name their type or the openers — the DISC hypothesis and the openers are what the Gem produces. You decide whom to prepare for, and what to tell it about the replies you have.

Start with the version the Gem itself suggests. Attach your Buying Center Map first, then describe how Lars replied to your first email:

Using the Buying Center Map I attached, prepare the human side of my meeting with Lars Meijer, Regional Operations Director. How he replied to the first email: within the hour, three short lines, no small talk — he asked only what a pilot on the three scrubber driers would involve and how quickly it could start.

When more than one stakeholder has replied, describe each reply and let the Gem prepare them all in one pass (or name just one and say the others are still silent, so it offers no type for the rest):

TipCopy into the Rapport Gem 
Using the Buying Center Map I attached, prepare me for everyone who has replied so far. Lars Meijer replied within the hour in three short lines, asking only about a pilot on the three scrubber driers. Sophie Verbeek in procurement replied two days later with a long list of questions about pricing, references, and contract terms.

Heading into a specific kind of meeting? Name the setting so the openers and what to listen for fit it:

TipCopy into the Rapport Gem

He replied to my first email within the hour, in three short lines, asking only what the pilot would involve and how fast it could start.

The Gem gets you ready; it does not run the conversation for you. You make that check yourself, in the room, not on the page.

B · Do it for real: your own account

3. Before your next real meeting this week, prepare one genuine opener and a type hypothesis with the Rapport Gem (attach your own buying-center map), then walk in without notes and just listen for the first two minutes.

As you debrief, ask the questions that matter:

  • Is the opener genuine, something you would actually say, or does it read as a performed line?
  • What single observation in the first two minutes would confirm or correct your DISC hypothesis?
  • Did you actually stop at the hand-off, or did the preparation keep talking for you?

35.6 In summary

This step prepares the human side of selling and then gets out of the way. You walk in with three things in place, confidence, conviction, and context, a fitting opening, a first read of the person as a working hypothesis, and a clear hand-off line where the tool stops and you take over. DISC gives you a way to read the type live and adapt your opening, pace, and detail, and that read steers how the needs analysis flows in the next step. Everything here is preparation; the trust and the listening happen in the room, which is why this short step matters as much as the research-heavy ones.

The next chapter, Needs Analysis, is where that preparation turns into sharper discovery questions. You carry two threads into it: the value hypothesis you came to test, and the DISC read that sets the pace, detail, and tone of the conversation with Lars and the wider Buying Center.

NoteWhat the research says

The chapter’s method has a firmer scientific footing than “rapport” usually gets credit for. The core skill, reading the person and adapting how you open, your pace, and your level of detail, is the peer-reviewed construct of adaptive selling. Weitz, Sujan and Sujan (1986) showed that effective salespeople adjust their behaviour to the customer and the situation, driven by two things: knowledge of customer types and fitting approaches, and the motivation to change their own behaviour. That is exactly what DISC operationalizes in this chapter. Spiro and Weitz (1990) then made adaptive selling measurable with the ADAPTS scale, and two of its five facets map straight onto this step: the confidence to alter your approach during a conversation and the collection of information to adapt. So “confidence” is not a mood; it is a component of a measured selling skill, and adapting your style is a learnable competence, not a personality gift.

Mindset earns its place here too. In a classic longitudinal study of MetLife sales agents, Seligman and Schulman (1986) found that a more optimistic explanatory style predicted higher sales productivity (the more optimistic half reportedly sold around 37% more) and a lower likelihood of quitting. The inner state you walk in with measurably shapes the result, which is why preparing the mindset is not a soft extra. (Treat the exact figure as the study’s reported result, not a guarantee.)

Rapport itself is a real, measurable thing rather than luck in the room. Gremler and Gwinner (2000) established rapport as a distinct construct with two dimensions, an enjoyable interaction and a personal connection, and linked it to satisfaction, loyalty intent, and word of mouth. That is the evidence behind preparing a genuine opening: rapport is built, and it carries the relationship. And the human half of the step, the listening, has its own validated construct, Active Empathetic Listening (Drollinger, Comer and Warrington 2006): precisely the competence the Gem hands back to you at the hand-off line, and the one the next chapter puts to work in full.

ImportantDISC, used honestly

DISC is a useful vocabulary, not a validated test, and the chapter treats it that way on purpose. The D/I/S/C labels trace back to Marston (1928), a pre-scientific work never designed as an assessment. When researchers compare the four-quadrant model to the Five-Factor Model (the Big Five, the validated structure in personality psychology; McCrae & John 1992), the DISC dimensions turn out to correlate with the Big Five rather than stand on their own (Jones & Hartley 2013). The honest reading: DISC is a fast, memorable way to observe and adapt live, but it is not a personality test and never a basis for AI-profiling someone from public data. That is exactly why we keep the type a hypothesis you confirm in the room.

NoteWhy the human moment still matters in the AI era

The market is moving the same way the chapter argues. Gartner (2025) forecasts that by 2030 roughly 75% of B2B buyers will prefer sales experiences that prioritize human interaction over AI, a reversal after years of growth in self-service and AI-driven buying, and strongest exactly where decisions are complex and high-stakes. AI front-loads and sharpens the preparation; the trust still gets built face to face. (A forecast to watch, not a settled number.)
