30  Pipeline-Steering: prioritise the whole book

Step 2 for leaders. Where a seller qualifies one lead, a leader prioritises the entire pipeline: the Pipeline-Steering Gem ranks the whole book by potential and flags the risk accounts and the follow-up gaps, straight from the Leadership Control Sheet.

This is the first of the three leadership moves. It reads the flat pipeline list in your Control Sheet and turns a vague quarter target into a focused set of moves for the week. Part of the Leadership Track.

30.1 What it does

At Step 2 the seller’s Account-Briefing Gem qualifies a single lead. The leadership equivalent applies that same qualification logic to the entire portfolio. The Pipeline-Steering Gem reads the flat pipeline list in your Control Sheet and does the comparison work for you: it ranks accounts by potential and flags the risk accounts and the potential accounts so they stop hiding in the middle of the list. You hand it two things — the portfolio in the sheet and your real target for the quarter — and it returns a short ranked list rather than a wall of data: the top accounts to push, the risk accounts that need attention now, and the follow-up gaps where momentum has quietly died. That turns a vague quarter target into a focused set of moves for the week, across the whole team’s book. A common use is the quarterly acquisition plan, derived from the same list. You stay the one who decides; the Gem just does the sorting and surfaces what you would otherwise miss.

30.2 Prompts you can copy and try

What you supply is the flat pipeline list from your Leadership Control Sheet (account, segment, stage, value, last contact, note) and your real target for the quarter; the ranking, the risk flags, and the follow-up gaps are what the Gem produces. Vary only the question you ask of the same connected sheet.

Start with the shortest version, the example prompt the Gem itself suggests, run against the pipeline you have connected:

TipCopy into the Pipeline-Steering Gem 
Rank this pipeline by potential and flag the risk accounts.

Give it your quarter target so the ranking sorts against what you actually need to hit:

TipCopy into the Pipeline-Steering Gem 
Here is my pipeline from the Leadership Control Sheet, and my target this quarter is to grow service revenue in the Benelux. Rank the whole book by potential against that target, and flag the risk accounts and the follow-up gaps separately. Use only what is in the list.

When the week is short, ask only for the accounts that have gone quiet, the fastest win in the sheet:

TipCopy into the Pipeline-Steering Gem 
From this pipeline, list only the stalled accounts: the ones with no recent contact and no defined next step. For each, give one line on why it stalled and the next steering action. Use only what is in the list.

Narrow to one segment or one owner when you want a focused view rather than the whole book:

TipCopy into the Pipeline-Steering Gem 
Rank only the BSC accounts in this pipeline by potential, including NorthStar Facility Services, and flag the risk accounts among them. Use only what is in the list.

Or ask for a short top list when you want the few accounts the team should push first:

TipCopy into the Pipeline-Steering Gem 
From this pipeline, give me the top five accounts by potential, with one line of reasoning each, plus the two risk accounts I should act on this week. Use only what is in the list.
NotePipeline-Steering Gem — full prompt 
Role: You are a portfolio-level pipeline analyst supporting a Kärcher Professional sales leader.
You reason about a whole book of accounts rather than a single deal, you keep every recommendation
transparent and explainable so the leader can sanity-check it, and you leave the steering call to
the leader.

Task: When the leader shares a flat pipeline list (one row per account, with columns such as
account, segment, stage, deal size or potential, last activity date, owner):
  1. Read where each account sits: its stage, how long it has been in that stage (age), and
     whether it is moving or stuck (momentum).
  2. Surface whitespace and unrealized potential — accounts or segments where Kärcher's equipment,
     service, and standardization could grow but the pipeline is thin.
  3. Prioritize the whole pipeline by potential, and flag the risk accounts (stuck, aging, or
     slipping) and the high-potential accounts explicitly.
  If a column the ranking depends on is missing or unreadable, say what you would need rather than
  guessing; work from the rest. If an optional fit reference (ideal customer profile, market
  context) is attached, judge potential and whitespace against it, not just the numbers in the sheet.

Context (what you already know): The leadership question is not "is this one lead worth my time?"
but "which accounts deserve the team's time first, where is the risk, and where is the untapped
potential?" The leader works from a flat list
(typically a connected Google Sheet), and the output feeds the Sales Leadership Control Sheet.
Prioritization is a hypothesis for the leader to confirm, not a verdict.

Format: Return a ranked overview ready to paste into the Leadership Control Sheet: each account
with its rank, a short reason for why it ranks where it does, and the next steering action. Group
or label the risk accounts and the high-potential accounts so they stand out. Return only the
ranked overview — no preamble. Respond in the same language the leader writes in.

Check:
- Work only from the pipeline data provided (and any attached fit reference); when a field is
  missing, name what you would need instead of inferring it. Keep each ranking explainable, so the
  leader can sanity-check the reasoning rather than trust a black box.
- Reserve a hard stop for what is costly if wrong: never invent accounts, numbers, or account
  history that the data does not contain.

The quality check before you act on it: is the ranking actually backed by what is in the sheet, or by a hopeful assumption? Are the risk accounts genuinely the ones you would worry about? Did the Gem flag missing data rather than fill it in? You keep the strongest part of the ranking, fix the weakest, and then steer.
