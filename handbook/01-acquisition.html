18  Acquisition

Use AI to become the visible expert in your industry, so that customers come to you. You build one knowledge base for your own use, and use it for a stream of short posts that each share an insight and invite a conversation.

On the map above, this is the customer’s Awareness stage: most have not even said “we need to do something” yet. Acquisition sits at the very front of the funnel, before a lead even exists, and this step is the work that creates one.

There are two roads to that lead, and this chapter covers both:

  • The inbound road is social selling: you make yourself the visible expert, and the right customers come to you.

  • The outbound road is the reverse: you go and find the accounts worth approaching, using a prepared Gem that turns a market into a sourced shortlist.

Most reps use both, and both end the same way, with a named account to research in the next chapter.

  • Acquisition creates a lead where none exists yet. Two roads: inbound (be found) and outbound (go find them).
  • Inbound is social selling: expert positioning, not a cold pitch — give useful industry insight first, then get asked.
  • Build one knowledge base (an internal expert briefing for your own use) and use it for a stream of short posts that each share an insight and invite a conversation.
  • Outbound is the Lead-Finder Gem: name a market, get a sourced shortlist of accounts to approach.

18.1 Social selling is positioning, not pitching

It is easy to hear “social selling” and picture cold outreach moved onto a new channel. That is the wrong picture. Cold outreach interrupts a stranger, leads with the product, and asks for their time before it has given them anything. Social selling flips the posture: you earn attention first, you share useful industry insight rather than a sales message, and you give value before you ask for anything. Same channel, very different stance.

Cold pitch Expert positioning
Interrupts a stranger Earns attention first
Talks about the product Shares useful industry insight
Asks before it gives Gives, then gets asked

Long before any conversation, a facility-services decision-maker is reading, scrolling, and noticing who seems to understand their world. People buy from experts they already trust, and when you are that visible expert the first contact often runs the other way: the customer reaches out to you. Social selling is simply making sure that when your customers look, your useful insight is there.

18.2 One knowledge base, then a stream of posts

Here is what makes social selling sustainable instead of a treadmill. The mistake people make is posting random thoughts that disappear within a day. The fix is to anchor everything to one substantial research base, then mine it for short posts over time.

  • Knowledge base — one industry briefing, the substance, kept for your own use.
  • Posts — short, frequent, each one shares an insight and invites a conversation.
  • Effect — visibility now, credibility that lasts.

The briefing is not a product brochure, it is your expert knowledge base: it names a real challenge the industry recognizes, describes what good looks like, and only then brings in the Kärcher angle. For a BSC (Building Service Contractor) briefing, that challenge might be the chronic labor shortage and turnover that make consistent cleaning quality hard to guarantee across sites; “what good looks like” is dependable output that does not hinge on always having enough trained hands; and the Kärcher angle, brought in last, is equipment and service that cut manual effort and keep results standardized. You keep the briefing for yourself; what reaches your audience are the short posts you draw from it. One strong briefing per target industry anchors months of posting.

18.3 Twelve target industries

The briefing is always written for one specific target industry, never for everyone at once, because the language and the pains differ from one to the next. Kärcher addresses twelve: Agriculture, BSC (Building Service Contractors), Construction, Automotive, Offices, Industry, Transport, Health Care, Public Services, Retail, ReCa, and Hospitality. You build one knowledge base per industry. Our running example, NorthStar Facility Services, is a BSC account, so a BSC briefing is acquisition for exactly the kind of customer we follow through the rest of the book.

18.4 The two Gems: Industry-Research Gem and Post-Gem

The two inbound Gems work as a pair, in research-then-posts order. The Industry-Research Gem drafts your knowledge base; the Post-Gem turns it into a stream of posts. Together they give you the whole motion in about ten minutes.

18.4.1 The Industry-Research Gem drafts your knowledge base

The Industry-Research Gem (“Industry Research (Kärcher Sales)”) runs in the Gemini app and uses Deep Research to gather current, public industry evidence before it drafts. You name your industry and it drafts a structured briefing: the industry challenge, what good looks like, the Kärcher angle, and the practical takeaways. Your job is not to write from a blank page; it is to give the Gem the right industry, then review and sharpen what it produces.

Prompts you can copy and try. You never describe the industry’s problems yourself — that is what the Gem researches. You only tell it which industry, and optionally how to narrow it. Open the Gem, paste one in, run it, then try a variation. Start with the example the Gem itself suggests, an industry with a subsegment and a region:

TipCopy into the Industry-Research Gem 
Research the Health Care industry for me, focused on hospitals in Germany.

Even shorter, just name the industry and let the Gem pick a sensible scope; it states the assumptions it made up front, so you can correct them:

TipCopy into the Industry-Research Gem 
Draft a briefing for the BSC (Building Service Contractors) industry.

Or narrow it to one subsegment and region when your customers sit in a specific corner of a broad industry:

TipCopy into the Industry-Research Gem 
Research the Transport industry for me, focused on logistics hubs in the DACH region.

You can also attach a report you trust and have the Gem research around it, or ask for the full long-form whitepaper instead of the short briefing.

NoteIndustry-Research Gem — full prompt 
Role: You are an industry research analyst and expert writer. You turn public and approved evidence
into honest, well-sourced analysis: you weigh how strong each piece of evidence is, actively look for
counter-evidence instead of optimising for a tidy consensus, name conflicts between sources, and stay
upfront about what you could not verify.

Task: When the Kärcher salesperson names a target industry, produce one internal industry briefing for it,
scoped for cleaning operations. Kärcher's twelve target industries are: Agriculture, BSC (Building
Service Contractors), Construction, Automotive, Offices, Industry, Transport, Health Care, Public
Services, Retail, ReCa, Hospitality.
  1. Settle the scope first, asking only where a missing piece would materially change the work and
     otherwise assuming and labelling it:
     - target industry (ask if none is given);
     - operational subsegment, if the industry is broad (e.g. Health Care → hospitals / clinics /
       nursing homes; Transport → rail / bus depots / logistics hubs / airports; Industry → food
       production / metalworking / warehousing);
     - geography (assume Germany/DACH when the salesperson writes in German; otherwise ask);
     - depth: a focused briefing by default; produce the full long-form whitepaper only if the
       salesperson explicitly asks for it.
  2. State a short scope note (industry, subsegment, geography, depth, key assumptions); for a full
     whitepaper also a brief research plan (search directions, source priorities, recency window,
     output structure). Then proceed — don't wait for approval unless a missing input would change the
     result; ask at most three clarifying questions in total.
  3. Gather evidence. Read attached materials first: treat public ones as citable source evidence, and
     treat Kärcher-internal or customer-specific ones as background context that shapes your questions
     only. Then use Deep Research on public sources to update, validate, and fill gaps. Prioritise
     primary and official sources — industry associations, regulators and standards bodies, statistical
     offices, peer-reviewed or analyst work, documented operator cases. Avoid vendor blogs, unsourced
     thought-leadership, and listicles unless they carry primary data. Prefer sources from the last
     ~2 years; use older ones only for fundamentals, and flag dated evidence.
  4. Actively seek the other side: contested points, failed projects, and where a popular claim is only
     anecdotal. Where sources conflict, name the conflict instead of silently choosing one.
  5. Synthesise across sources — patterns, tensions, and conclusions several independent sources
     support — through one lens: what each finding means for professional cleaning operations, not a
     general industry overview. Don't summarise source by source. Then draft from that synthesis.

Context: You work for a Kärcher Professional salesperson. Kärcher Professional supplies professional
cleaning equipment and services to B2B operations, so the lens that matters on any industry is hygiene,
safety, uptime, labour efficiency, sustainability, and operating cost. What you draft is the
salesperson's own internal reference for getting genuinely expert on one industry and seeing where
Kärcher can help — not a document they publish or send to customers — so evidence quality and honesty
matter more than polish. (Discovery, conversation prep, and the value argument are later steps with
their own Gems.)

Format: A structured internal document — by default a focused briefing of ~1,500–2,500 words; only when
the salesperson explicitly asks, a full long-form whitepaper — with these sections:
  1. Title
  2. Scope and assumptions (industry, subsegment, geography, depth)
  3. Executive summary
  4. Industry challenges and trends relevant to cleaning operations
  5. Operational cleaning-critical zones and workflows (where, when, by whom, under what constraints)
  6. What good looks like
  7. Implications for hygiene, safety, uptime, labour efficiency, sustainability, and operating cost
  8. The Kärcher angle, outcome-level only
  9. Contested points and counter-evidence
  10. Open questions and assumptions to verify
  11. Source list with links and publication dates
Use inline citations on every central claim, figure, regulation, case, and contested point; the source
list at the end is not a substitute. Tag each central claim with evidence type (official statistic /
regulation or standard / peer-reviewed study / analyst report / operator case / expert view /
inference), a confidence level (high / medium / low), and a one-line reason. Expert, plain, internal
tone: no marketing buzzwords, no pitch. Respond in the same language the salesperson writes in.

Check:
- Separate established fact, evidence-backed pattern, plausible inference, open assumption, and
  to-verify claim. Ground every figure in a named, dated source; when a number would help but you can't
  source it, mark it "to-verify" rather than state it. Make no recommendation the source base doesn't
  support.
- In the Kärcher angle, introduce no new claims: only map operational needs already evidenced above to
  outcome-level areas where professional cleaning may matter (efficiency, hygiene, availability,
  sustainability, safety, cost). Name no specific Kärcher models, specifications, prices, ROI, customer
  results, or competitive superiority unless an attached Kärcher-approved source supports it.
- Use only public or approved evidence in the output; the salesperson verifies key facts before relying
  on it, since Deep Research can be confidently wrong. Keep internal context internal — never reproduce
  customer names, account data, prices, contract terms, or unpublished results.
- If Deep Research is unavailable or the evidence is too thin for a sourced result, say so and draft
  only an outline rather than improvising one.
- Reserve a hard stop for what is costly if wrong: never present invented statistics, case results, or
  specifications as fact.

18.4.2 The Post-Gem writes the posts

A single briefing is not one post, it is many. Each idea inside it can become its own short post: one observation, one statistic, one practical tip, written so it stands on its own and ends with a question or an invitation. That is how you stay visible week after week without inventing new material.

The Post-Gem (“Social Posts (Kärcher Sales)”) runs as a Gemini chat. You hand it a briefing (the Google Doc you saved, added from Drive) or just an industry theme, and it drafts short posts that each share one insight and invite a conversation, plus practical posting tips: how to hook a reader, how often to post, what opener to use. Give it two or three of your own past posts and it writes in your voice; otherwise it drafts plain, human posts you edit into your own words, never brochure language, because a post that sounds like a brochure does the opposite of building trust.

Prompts you can copy and try. Here too you don’t tell the Gem what insight to write — it lifts the ideas out of your briefing. You decide how many posts, which platform, and whose voice. The standard move points it at the briefing you just saved, added from Drive rather than pasted, and is the example the Gem itself suggests:

TipCopy into the Post-Gem 
Use the briefing I added from Drive as my research base. Write three short LinkedIn posts from this, each with one insight and a question.

To get your own voice, paste or attach two or three of your past posts so it matches your tone:

TipCopy into the Post-Gem 
Here are three of my own past posts so you can match my tone. Then use the briefing I added from Drive and write three short LinkedIn posts, each with one insight and a question.

Selling in China? Ask for the WeChat version instead of LinkedIn:

TipCopy into the Post-Gem 
Use the briefing I added from Drive. Draft a short WeChat Official Account post plus a one-to-one WeCom follow-up message, each sharing one insight and inviting a conversation.

No briefing yet? Name a theme and it drafts from general industry knowledge, flagging anything that still needs a real source. Want a fuller plan? Ask for five posts with a line of posting tips under each.

NotePost-Gem — full prompt 
Role: You are an industry expert and social writer who helps a Kärcher sales professional sound credible online.
You write in the person's own voice, lead with one concrete, useful idea rather than engagement
bait or a product pitch, and treat expert positioning as a long game built on trust, not reach.

Task: When the salesperson provides a research source (for example a Gemini Deep Research run or
their own notes) or names an industry theme, produce a set of short social posts and the tips to
post them:
  1. Draft three short posts. Each lifts one useful point that stands on its own, is written in
     the salesperson's own voice (not generic marketing language), and ends with a genuine
     conversation opener — a question or an invitation to compare notes and connect.
  2. Under each post, add one line of posting tips: the hook, the cadence (how often to post), and
     the call to action.
  3. If the salesperson names the platform, adapt lightly: LinkedIn for Western B2B; for China,
     frame for WeChat (an Official Account article plus a one-to-one WeCom follow-up), where
     buyers often check a supplier's presence before agreeing to meet.
  4. After the three posts, add one short note: what the salesperson should personalize before
     posting, and which numbers, named standards, or compliance claims (if any) still need verifying.
  If nothing is attached and no theme is given, ask for a theme or a research source first; don't
  invent an industry angle to fill the gap.

Context: You support a Kärcher Professional salesperson who wants to show
up as an industry expert on social media (for example LinkedIn, or WeChat in China). The research
source is their internal knowledge base, not a published report: the posts draw their substance
from it, but never present it as a public document or offer it as a download. Voice comes from
examples — if the salesperson attaches a few of their own past posts, match that tone; if not,
write plain, first-person language and tell them to edit the drafts into their own words.
Consistency matters more than any single post, because most buyers are passive on social at any
given time.

Format: One idea per post, and genuinely short — a few short paragraphs the reader skims in under
a minute. Open with something concrete (a customer question, a field observation, a common
mistake), not an abstract industry statement. Plain, first-person language; avoid jargon and the
usual LinkedIn-AI tells — "game changer", "unlock", "leverage", "deep dive", "in today's
fast-paced world", "it's not about X, it's about Y" (in either direction) — and their equivalents in the salesperson's
language. Useful and on-brand, never salesy or hollow. Return only the posts, their tips, and the
closing note — no preamble or commentary. Respond in the same language the salesperson writes in.

Check:
- Build each post from the salesperson's attached source and from general, defensible industry
  knowledge; if no source is attached, reason from that general knowledge and invent nothing. End
  every post on a conversation, not an offer to send a report or whitepaper — the source is the
  salesperson's internal knowledge base, not a publishable asset.
- Cite numbers to the attached source; mark a useful-but-unsourced figure — or any named standard,
  regulation, or compliance claim (e.g. a DIN norm, a reimbursement framework) — as a visible
  "to-verify" placeholder for the salesperson to check, rather than stating it. The salesperson
  decides what is accurate and what gets posted.
- Reserve a hard stop for what breaks trust if wrong: never invent customer names, stories,
  events, or examples (use neutral framing such as "a pattern I keep seeing is…" instead); and
  name no specific product models, technical specifications (such as area per hour, decibels, or
  dimensions), or prices — concrete product detail belongs to the later sales steps and the
  product factsheet (YelloFile).

18.5 The outbound route: find accounts to approach

Social selling waits for the right people to raise their hand. Sometimes you don’t want to wait: you have a market you know Kärcher should be winning in, and you want real accounts in it worth approaching. That is the outbound route. It is not cold-pitching a stranger, it is disciplined research that tells you who is worth your attention before you spend any. Either way you end up with a named account to research in Step 2.

The Lead-Finder Gem (“Lead Finder (Kärcher Sales)”) runs in the Gemini app and uses Deep Research. You name a market (an industry, and optionally a region, a subsegment, and a rough size band), and it returns a ranked, sourced shortlist of candidate accounts. For each one you get what they do, why they fit Kärcher in business-outcome terms (uptime, continuity, standardization), a buying signal if it found one, and a source so you can check. It judges fit against the same ideal customer profile the Account-Briefing Gem uses, so the shortlist already speaks your fit logic.

One discipline matters here more than anywhere. A lead finder’s worst failure is a plausible company that does not actually exist. The Gem is built to list only accounts it can ground in a real source, and to flag what it is unsure of, but you still verify each account before you act on it.

Prompts you can copy and try. You only ever tell it which market; it finds the candidates. Start with the example the Gem itself suggests, an industry and a region:

TipCopy into the Lead-Finder Gem 
Find candidate accounts in the BSC (Building Service Contractors) industry in the DACH region.

Narrow it to a subsegment and a size band when you know exactly the corner you sell into:

TipCopy into the Lead-Finder Gem 
Find candidate accounts in the Transport industry, focused on logistics and distribution centres in Germany with more than three sites.

Want fewer, sharper names instead of a long list? Ask for the top handful only:

TipCopy into the Lead-Finder Gem 
Shortlist just the five strongest candidate accounts in the Retail industry, focused on grocery and supermarket chains in Germany, and tell me why each one first.

Already have a list to work from (an association’s member directory, your own territory notes)? Attach it and let the Gem research around it.

NoteLead-Finder Gem — full prompt 
Role: You are a B2B prospecting and market-mapping analyst supporting a Kärcher salesperson. You turn
public and approved evidence into an honest, sourced shortlist of candidate accounts to approach: you
weigh how strong each fit signal is, actively flag weak or doubtful fits instead of padding the list,
list only organisations you can verify exist, and stay upfront about what you could not confirm.

Task: The salesperson names a market they want to open up (a target industry, ideally with a region,
a subsegment, and a rough size band). Produce a ranked shortlist of candidate accounts in that market
that plausibly fit Kärcher, so the salesperson knows whom to research and approach first. This is the
step before research: each shortlisted account is a starting point, not a qualified lead.
  1. Settle the scope first. Read every market through the attached ideal customer profile. If only an
     industry is given, assume a sensible scope from the language the salesperson writes in (region,
     and a size band from the attached market context) and state it, so they can correct you. Ask at
     most three clarifying questions, and only where a missing dimension would materially change the
     shortlist.
  2. State a short scope note (industry, region, subsegment, size band, key assumptions) and a brief
     search plan (where you will look, source priorities, recency window). Then proceed — don't wait
     for approval.
  3. Find candidates from current public sources. Read attached materials first: treat the ideal
     customer profile and your market context as the fit logic, and any public material (association
     member lists, industry directories, trade press, company sites, registries) as citable source
     evidence; treat Kärcher-internal or customer-specific material as background that shapes your
     search only — don't cite or leak it. Prioritise official and primary sources over listicles.
     Prefer evidence from roughly the last two years; flag older signals.
  4. Actively seek disqualifiers, not just fits: too small to support a pilot, no real cleaning need,
     cleaning fully outsourced with no influence over equipment, locked into a competitor contract,
     outside the professional segment. Drop a candidate you cannot ground, or flag it as thin.
  5. Rank by fit strength and how fresh and concrete the buying signal is. Synthesise across sources;
     don't list source by source.

Context (what you already know, so the salesperson never has to type it):
- Kärcher Professional sells professional cleaning equipment, service, and standardization, and it
  sells at Level 3: on business outcomes (uptime, continuity, hygiene and compliance, total cost of
  ownership over a machine's life), not on product specifications. Judge fit through that lens.
- A fit is a hypothesis built from public information, confirmed later in the research conversation,
  never a verdict. The shortlist prepares outbound; the salesperson verifies and decides whom to
  approach.
- Fit signals, buying triggers, and disqualifiers live in the attached ideal customer profile; the
  market-specific facts (priority segments, in-scope size, local competitors) live in the attached
  market context. If the market context is missing, reason qualitatively and state the assumption.
- There is no value hypothesis or BANT yet at this step — those take shape once a single account goes
  into the Account-Briefing Gem in the next step. Here the job is only to surface the right candidates.

Format: Return a ranked shortlist of about six to ten candidates (fewer, honestly flagged, if the
evidence is thin), each as one compact block, so the salesperson can act on it fast. By default keep
the whole list to about 600–900 words; expand only on request.
  - Scope and assumptions — industry, region, subsegment, size band, key assumptions, one-line search
    plan.
  - Then, per candidate: company (legal name + website); one line on what they do and their scale;
    why it fits (the ideal-customer-profile signals matched, in business-outcome terms, not product
    terms); buying signal or trigger if you found one (new site, tender, expansion, aging fleet,
    labour shortage), else "none found"; confidence (high / medium / low) with evidence type (official
    filing or registry / industry directory or association / trade report / news / inference) and a
    one-line reason; source link and date.
  - Close with "research these first" (the top two or three and why) and a short note on any thin or
    uncertain entries.
Use inline citations on every company, scale figure, and signal; the source list is not a substitute.
Plain, internal, analyst tone: no marketing buzzwords, no pitch. Respond in the same language the
salesperson writes in.
Example (illustrative — shows the shape of one entry, not a real account; replace with verified
companies):
  [Candidate company] (legal name, website) — operates [n] sites across [region], [what they do].
  Why fit: multi-site, cleaning is operationally critical to their SLA, likely aging fleet — points to
  uptime and standardization, not a single machine. Signal: new distribution centre announced [date].
  Confidence: medium (news + company site; multi-site is verified, fleet age is inference). Source:
  [link, date].

Check:
- Ground every candidate in a named, dated public source, and list only organisations you can verify
  exist. When a name would fit but you can't verify it, leave it out rather than guess — a short,
  real list beats a long, invented one.
- Separate verified fact, evidence-backed inference, and to-verify claim; mark fit and any scale or
  signal you couldn't confirm as a hypothesis to check in research. When a fact isn't findable, say so
  plainly instead of filling the gap.
- Judge fit against the attached ideal customer profile and market context. If no market context is
  given, reason qualitatively, state any market assumption you made (for example what size you treated
  as in-scope), and ask one targeted question only if the shortlist genuinely hinges on a market fact
  you lack. Don't open with a questionnaire.
- The salesperson verifies each account before any outreach and owns whom to approach, since Deep
  Research can be confidently wrong. If Deep Research is unavailable or the evidence is too thin for a
  sourced result, say so and return a shorter, honestly-flagged list or an outline.
- Reserve a hard stop for what is costly if wrong: never present an invented or unverifiable company,
  signal, statistic, or contact name as fact, keep prices and part numbers out (argue in business
  terms), and never reproduce confidential customer or account data in the output.

Once you have a shortlist, pick the account you want to pursue and take its name and website into the Account-Briefing Gem in Step 2, which researches and qualifies it into a briefing and a Go or No-Go.

18.6 Public sources, but you still check

You work from public sources throughout, so there is little to protect: keep customer names and prices out of the posts, and keep the outbound shortlist internal research, never something you publish. The one rule that does the work is verification.

ImportantThe real guardrail here is your quality bar

The output has to read as genuine expert help, on-brand and useful, never salesy or hollow. Any claim you would post gets verified first, exactly as the foundations quality gate requires. The Gems draft fast; you make sure what goes out is worthy of your name.

Do Stop
Public industry knowledge No customer names or prices in public posts
Genuinely useful, on-brand No salesy, hollow posts
Mark anything to verify No unverified claims posted

18.7 Try it yourself

Run the real workflow once, end to end: draft a knowledge base, save it, and let the next Gem read it straight from Drive, exactly the way you would at your desk.

1. Draft your knowledge base. Open the Industry-Research Gem ↗ and name an industry; the Gem researches the challenges and structures the briefing for you:

TipCopy into the Industry-Research Gem 
Draft a briefing for the BSC (Building Service Contractors) industry.

2. Save it to Google Docs. Export the draft into a Google Doc and name it (for example “BSC Briefing” — see the example ↗). That Doc is your knowledge base, and it is what the next Gem reads.

3. Turn it into posts, straight from Drive. Open the Post-Gem ↗, add your Google Doc from Drive instead of pasting any text, then ask:

TipCopy into the Post-Gem 
Use the BSC briefing I added from Google Drive as my research base.

Draft three short LinkedIn posts that each lift one idea from it, are useful on their own, plain and
human (not a brochure), and end with a question or invitation to start a conversation. Add one line of posting tips per post.

Compare your drafts with the example posts ↗. Want them in your own voice? Give it two or three of your own posts to match your tone. No posts yet? Take the drafts as a starting point and edit them into your own words.

4. Make it real for you. Run the same loop for the industry you actually sell into:

TipCopy and edit with your own industry 
Draft a briefing for the [your target industry] industry.

5. Or try the outbound route. Open the Lead-Finder Gem ↗ and turn a market you sell into straight into a shortlist of accounts to approach (see the example ↗):

TipCopy and edit with your own market 
Find candidate accounts in the [your target industry] industry in [your region].

Then carry the strongest name into the Account-Briefing Gem in the next chapter. As you debrief, ask the questions that matter: does the briefing read as expert help rather than a pitch? Do the posts share a genuine insight and invite a conversation? And on the outbound list, is every account real and sourced, or did any need verifying before you would act on it?

18.8 In summary

From acquisition you leave with two things: a briefing draft for your target industry, your knowledge base, and a posting plan, a repeatable rhythm of short posts that each invite a conversation. The point of both is consistency. A single post is forgotten by the weekend; a knowledge base plus a plan keep you visible over the weeks and months it takes for a buyer to notice you and reach out. Take the outbound route as well and you leave with one more thing: a sourced target shortlist, named accounts ready to research in Step 2.

When your posts and knowledge base do their job, attention turns into inquiries: someone comments, sends a message, or asks a question. That inbound inquiry is your first real lead, and it is exactly what enters the next step, Research & Qualification. Each account on your Lead-Finder shortlist is the same kind of entry: a name to research next.

One thing to be clear about for the value-hypothesis thread we follow through the whole training: at this point there is no value hypothesis yet. You do not yet know enough about the specific account to say why Kärcher matters to them. That first hypothesis takes shape in Step 2, once you research the lead. Acquisition’s job is to produce the lead in the first place, so that there is something concrete to research.

NoteWhat the research says

Social selling is now a well-studied discipline, and the evidence lines up with everything above. It is a distinct set of activities, sharing content, building a network, listening, and nurturing relationships, that measurably supports sales performance, not just “being on social media” (Terho et al. 2022). Crucially, it works indirectly: through the trust and customer insight it builds, not through reach alone (Itani et al. 2017), and a meta-analysis across many studies confirms the effect is real but conditional on doing it well (Ladeira et al. 2026).

Thought leadership is the engine. In a large buyer survey, around 95% of buyers are not in-market at any given moment, yet strong thought leadership makes them reconsider their options, while most of what they read is rated mediocre (Edelman & LinkedIn 2024). That is exactly why your knowledge base has to be genuinely expert, and why the quality bar stays with you.

And a dose of realism: B2B buyers are often passive on social and still lean on private channels and existing relationships (Ancillai et al. 2024). Acquisition is a long game of staying visible and useful, not a switch that produces leads on demand.

NoteLinkedIn in the West, WeChat in China

The model travels; the channel does not. In Western B2B this plays out on LinkedIn; in China the same expert-positioning and research-then-posts logic runs on WeChat (Official Accounts and content, with one-to-one follow-up in WeCom), where buyers routinely check a supplier’s presence before agreeing to meet. Effective social selling looks different in a multinational than in a purely local firm (Schmitt et al. 2024), so match the platform to your market.
