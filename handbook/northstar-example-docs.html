Appendix B — NorthStar Worked Example: The Documents

Every Gemini output the NorthStar example produces across the sales cycle — each one a live Google Doc you can open

Throughout the book, the NorthStar example shows each step producing a real working document — a briefing, a buying-center map, a need profile, a value block, and so on — which the next step then picks up. This appendix collects those documents in one place, in the order the cycle creates them.

Each one is a simulated Gemini Gem output: an example of what the prepared Gem would return, written so you can see the shape of a good result before you make your own. None of it is confidential customer data — NorthStar is a fictional account, figures are either marked to-verify or come from the internal BR 45/22 YelloFile, and no real prices appear.

Every document below is also a live Google Doc, shared read-only, in the Drive Resources folder. Where a recipe in the book offers a ready-made Doc to practise on, this is the Doc it links. The Docs are read-only, so the move is always the same: open it, make a copy to your Drive (File → Make a copy), and attach the copy.

BSC Briefing

📄 Open the live Google Doc ↗

Simulated Industry-Research Gem output — the rep’s internal knowledge base for the acquisition step, not a publishable lead magnet. It names the challenge, what good looks like, the Kärcher angle, and the practical takeaways for one target industry. You keep this for yourself and draw short posts from it. Every number is marked to-verify — verify any claim before you rely on it, and name no specific product models in what reaches your audience.

Target industry: BSC (Building Service Contractors) — facility-services providers in the Benelux.

The challenge

Building service contractors sell an outcome they have to deliver every day on someone else’s premises: consistent cleaning quality across many client sites, on thin margins, with teams that are hard to hire and harder to keep. Chronic labor shortage and high turnover mean the people doing tonight’s shift may not be the people who did last week’s, while clients judge every site against the same contract standard. Equipment that breaks down mid-shift or takes long to learn turns that daily pressure into visible quality gaps — and in this business, a quality gap is a contract risk. (Scale of labor shortage and turnover in the Benelux market: to-verify with current sources.)

What good looks like

  • The same standard on every site — quality the client can rely on, regardless of which team is on.
  • Output that does not hinge on always having enough trained hands — dependable results under staffing pressure.
  • Fast onboarding — new operators productive and safe on the machines quickly, despite turnover.
  • Uptime across a dispersed fleet — a failure on a client site costs quality, hours, and trust at once.

The Kärcher angle

The strongest position is not a machine pitch but service-backed standardization: reliable, easy-to-learn equipment plus service and training support that keep quality consistent across sites and shifts. The story is uptime, standardization, and service continuity — the contractor’s own promise to its clients, made easier to keep — rather than a feature list.

Practical takeaways

  • Lead with the consistency-across-sites problem; it is the contractor’s own selling promise, felt daily.
  • Treat service and training as part of the value, not an add-on — turnover makes them decisive.
  • Keep claims verifiable — contractors calculate sharply, and unsupported figures cost credibility.
  • Use this briefing as your research base for short posts; share one insight at a time, never the whole document.

BSC Posting Plan

📄 Open the live Google Doc ↗

Simulated Post-Gem output, drawn from the BSC Briefing. Each post lifts one insight from the briefing into its own short piece and ends with a genuine question that invites a conversation — not a pitch. Edit them into your own voice before posting; share one insight at a time, never the whole briefing. No product is named, and no figure is stated as fact.

Post 1 — the same standard on every site

A building service contractor’s hardest promise isn’t cleaning one site well — it’s delivering the same standard on thirty sites, with thirty different teams, every night.

Clients rarely see the effort behind that consistency. They only see the one site where it slipped.

What does it take in your operation to keep site number thirty as good as site number one?

Post 2 — turnover is an operations problem

When teams change faster than routines can settle, every process has to survive being handed to someone new. That’s not just a training question — it shapes which tools, machines, and routines are even workable.

The operations that cope best seem to be the ones designed for handover from the start.

How do you make sure quality survives a team change on your sites?

Post 3 — a breakdown on a client’s premises

When a machine fails mid-shift in your own facility, it costs you time. When it fails on a client’s premises, it costs you the thing you sell: reliability.

That difference changes how equipment decisions get made in contract cleaning — uptime and service response weigh as much as raw performance.

Where does an unplanned breakdown hurt your business most — the schedule, the budget, or the client relationship?

Posting plan · post on a steady rhythm; the first reply is your first lead.

NorthStar Buying Center Map

📄 Open the live Google Doc ↗

Simulated Buying-Center Gem output, built from the NorthStar Account Briefing. Every entry is a hypothesis to verify, not a fact: each comes with the one question that would confirm or kill it. The point of the map is not to look finished — it is to make the missing voices visible before the first conversation.

Account and opportunity

  • Account: NorthStar Facility Services Benelux (Rotterdam, industrial and logistics facility services)
  • Opportunity: a pilot of three Kärcher BR 45/22 C Bp Pack battery scrubber driers, plus operator onboarding and a service-start package; a broader rollout opens only if the pilot proves out.
  • Value hypothesis carried from research: standardizing the cleaning fleet across NorthStar’s sites (equipment and service) could cut avoidable downtime and disruption during shift-change and incident windows.

The four roles

Lars Meijer — Regional Operations Director (Rotterdam)

  • In the buying center: decision-maker / sponsor.
  • Goal: uptime and a smooth, low-disruption rollout he can stand behind.
  • Pain: mid-shift breakdowns on a mixed, aging fleet create escalations and customer pressure.
  • Opening angle: reliability and pilot credibility — proof the pilot reduces disruption across sites.
  • Confirm-or-kill question: Which single site is the most credible pilot location right now, and what result would justify a broader rollout?

Sophie Verbeek — Procurement Manager (Rotterdam)

  • In the buying center: purchasing / commercial gatekeeper.
  • Goal: a clear, defendable scope where the service component is justified, not just a higher price.
  • Pain: being handed a machine-only comparison she cannot defend internally.
  • Opening angle: scope clarity and a service logic that holds up against a unit-price comparison.
  • Confirm-or-kill question: What does the commercial review need to see for the service component to be defendable, not just the unit price?

Milan Bakker — Site Service Supervisor (Breda cluster)

  • In the buying center: user.
  • Goal: equipment and routines that work under pressure, without three different workarounds per site.
  • Pain: onboarding and daily handling that feel impractical on the floor; if it is awkward, confidence drops fast.
  • Opening angle: daily usability and operator routine — fewer workarounds across locations.
  • Confirm-or-kill question: Where exactly do the current machines fail mid-shift, and what would make a new routine simpler for operators?

Eva van Dijk — HSE and Service Coordination Lead (Rotterdam)

  • In the buying center: secondary, but can block a rollout.
  • Goal: a safe rollout with a clear support and escalation path.
  • Pain: unclear response ownership when a unit goes down.
  • Opening angle: onboarding quality, escalation clarity, and response ownership.
  • Confirm-or-kill question: When a unit fails on site, who owns the response today, and what response time would you need to back a rollout?

How to approach the account

  • Reach first: Lars (the sponsor). He replied fast and direct; he opens the door to the others.
  • Bring in early: Milan, so the floor-level reality shapes the offer before procurement engages.
  • Engage once a workable concept exists: Sophie, with the scope and service logic already clear.
  • Confirm before any rollout commitment: Eva, on safety, training, and escalation.

What is still thin (to verify)

  • Whether anyone else holds budget authority above Sophie.
  • Whether an incumbent equipment supplier is already locked in (none found in public research).
  • Who, between Lars and Sophie, owns final commercial sign-off for the pilot.

NorthStar First-Contact Email

📄 Open the live Google Doc ↗

Simulated First-Contact Gem output, built from the NorthStar Buying Center Map. The Gem leads with the right pressure for each role (Purpose / Benefit / Check) and keeps the email a short door-opener, not a pitch. Sender: Martin Bauer, Kärcher Professional. Drafted for review — read it against the three checks before you would ever send it.

Email to Lars Meijer — Regional Operations Director (decision-maker)

Subject: Three scrubber driers, one pilot — less mid-shift downtime at NorthStar

Dear Mr Meijer,

You run a mixed fleet across many sites, and when a machine fails mid-shift the operational impact is immediate. That is the problem worth fixing first.

The idea is small and low-risk: a pilot of three Kärcher BR 45/22 C Bp Pack battery scrubber driers at one site, with operator onboarding and service support included, so you can see whether a standardized, serviced fleet actually cuts avoidable downtime before anyone talks about a wider rollout.

Would a short call in the next two weeks make sense, to agree which site would be the most credible place to prove it?

Kind regards, Martin Bauer Kärcher Professional

Variant for Sophie Verbeek — Procurement Manager (purchasing)

Subject: Pilot scope at NorthStar — built to compare on more than unit price

Dear Ms Verbeek,

When a cleaning-equipment proposal lands on your desk, the hard part is defending it internally as more than a unit-price comparison.

For the NorthStar pilot we would set out a clear, defendable scope: three BR 45/22 C Bp Pack scrubber driers plus onboarding and a service-start package, with the service component spelled out so its value is visible, not assumed.

Would it help to walk through the scope together before the commercial review, so the comparison is on the right basis from the start?

Kind regards, Martin Bauer Kärcher Professional

Before sending — the three checks

  1. A real pressure? Yes — mid-shift downtime (Lars) and a defendable scope (Sophie) are their actual concerns, not generic flattery.
  2. Still a door-opener? Each email asks for one short conversation, not a decision.
  3. Would you sign it yourself? If any line reads like a brochure, cut it before it goes out.

NorthStar Meeting Prep

📄 Open the live Google Doc ↗

Simulated Rapport Gem output, built from the NorthStar Buying Center Map plus a note on how Lars replied to the first email. It prepares the human side of the meeting — the opener, what to listen for, and a personality hypothesis to test, not a script. Read it as a starting point, then stop at the hand-off line and listen.

Who and context

  • Meeting with: Lars Meijer, Regional Operations Director, NorthStar (the decision-maker / sponsor).
  • How he replied to the first email: within the hour, three short lines, no small talk — he asked only what a pilot on the three scrubber driers would involve and how quickly it could start.

Personality hypothesis (to confirm in the room, never to announce)

The reply reads direct and results-driven — a fast, dominant style: short on small talk, focused on outcome and speed. Treat this as a hypothesis. If it holds, match it: be brief, lead with the result, respect his time. If it does not, adjust.

The opener

Open on his terms, not yours:

“Thanks for making time. You asked how fast a pilot could start and what it involves — I’d like to answer that directly, and check I’ve understood where downtime hurts you most. Can I start with two quick questions?”

No warm-up, no company history. One sentence of purpose, then hand the floor over.

What to listen for in the first two minutes

The one signal that confirms or corrects the hypothesis: does he drive the pace and push for the outcome, or does he slow down and want detail and reassurance?

  • If he drives → stay brief, talk results and timing, propose a concrete next step early.
  • If he slows down → give more structure and proof, and do not rush the close.

Either way, listen for where exactly downtime hurts and what result would justify a rollout — that is the thread into discovery.

The hand-off line

Stop here and let him talk:

“Before I say anything about the machines — where does the current fleet let you down most: shift changes, incidents, or high-throughput periods?”

After that line, your job is to listen and capture, not to present. The discovery questions take over.

If the hypothesis is wrong

If he opens with small talk, wants the broader story, or brings others into the room, drop the brisk-and-direct approach: slow down, widen the picture, and read whether the real priority is his or the group’s.

NorthStar Discovery Questions

📄 Open the live Google Doc ↗

Simulated Needs-Analysis Gem output (drafting job), built from the NorthStar Account Briefing and the Buying Center Map. The questions follow the SNIP order — Situation → Need → Impact → Pay-off — and are deliberately aligned to the value hypothesis, so the conversation tests what you actually believe. This is a conversation guide to edit, not a script to read out. Cut it to the one or two questions per step you would genuinely ask, and delete any that quietly presuppose the solution.

Meeting: Lars Meijer (Regional Operations Director) and Milan Bakker (Site Service Supervisor). Value hypothesis under test: a reliable, serviced fleet — starting with a low-risk pilot on three BR 45/22 C Bp Pack scrubber driers — would cut downtime and simplify daily operations.

Situation — understand the current state

  • Which sites carry the heaviest cleaning load, and how is the work split across shifts? (Lars)
  • What machines are in use today across those sites, and how standardized are they? (Milan)

Need — surface the concrete problems

  • Where exactly does the current fleet let you down — shift changes, incidents, or high-throughput periods? (Lars, high-gain)
  • When a routine differs from site to site, what goes wrong for the operators? (Milan, high-gain)

Impact — make the cost of inaction visible

  • When a unit fails mid-shift, what is the knock-on effect — on readiness, on your own customers, on escalations? (Lars)
  • How much time goes into workarounds and re-cleaning when equipment or routines are inconsistent? (Milan)

Pay-off — make the value of the target state tangible

  • If a serviced, standardized pilot fleet held up through the worst windows, what would change for you operationally? (Lars)
  • What would make a new routine feel genuinely simpler on the floor, not just different? (Milan)
  • What result from a single-site pilot would justify a broader rollout? (Lars, high-gain)

Notes for the conversation

  • Lead with one or two questions per step; let silence do the work.
  • Do not name the solution inside a question. “A serviced fleet” is fine; “our BR 45/22” is not.
  • Capture the answers in your own words during the visit — that Doc becomes the need profile next.

The empty boxes are the point — they are your follow-up list, not boxes to fill with a guess. This profile is what you attach to the Value-Argument Gem next.

“If one unit is down, the operational impact is immediate.” Mixed, aging fleet; mid-shift failures hit site readiness directly.

Reliability and uptime are the clearest, most-repeated need. Whose: Lars.

Service — strong

Support, responsiveness, and continuity carry real weight. Whose: Eva (and Lars). Note: the escalation detail itself is still thin — see Safety.

Economy — weak (logic present, figures missing)

The commercial logic is named, but there are no cost figures or a defendable scope yet. Whose: Sophie (not in the room).

Comfort — underexplored

Operators improvise when a machine is down or unfamiliar; onboarding is uneven. Touched, not explored.

Daily usability and routine simplification barely covered. Whose: Milan.

Safety — underexplored

Eva joined for 15 minutes; wanted the escalation path and training handling, but we did not go deep.

Escalation ownership and response time when a unit fails are not yet pinned down. Whose: Eva.

Image — underexplored / not discussed

The empty boxes — one follow-up each

  • Comfort gap → ask Milan about operator onboarding and routine simplification across sites.
  • Safety gap → ask Eva about the escalation path and support response when a unit fails.

Priority for the value argument

Build the argument on the confirmed strong needs first — Performance (uptime) and Service (responsiveness/continuity) — then make Economy defendable with a clear scope. Do not argue Comfort, Safety, or Image as if they were confirmed; carry them as open questions until the evidence is in.

NorthStar Value Block

📄 Open the live Google Doc ↗

Simulated Value-Argument Gem output, built from the NorthStar Need Profile and the BR 45/22 YelloFile. The chains follow FABC — Feature → Advantage → Benefit → Check — and are ordered by the needs the customer confirmed most strongly. Every benefit traces back to a confirmed need; anything that does not map was dropped. Product facts come from the YelloFile, and any figure is flagged to-verify until you have checked it.

Offer: three Kärcher BR 45/22 C Bp Pack battery scrubber driers, plus operator onboarding and a service-start package. Roles prepared for: Lars Meijer (Operations), Sophie Verbeek (Procurement).

Chain 1 — Performance / uptime (strongest confirmed need)

  • Feature: battery-powered scrubber drier with consistent power delivery and no trailing cable (spec detail from the YelloFile — to-verify).
  • Advantage: consistent cleaning result with fewer mid-shift interruptions than an aging, mixed fleet.
  • Benefit: less avoidable downtime in the windows that hurt most (shift changes, incidents), so site readiness holds. Maps to: “If one unit is down, the operational impact is immediate.”
  • Check: “If mid-shift stoppages dropped at the pilot site, would that be the proof you need for a rollout?”

Chain 2 — Service / continuity (strong confirmed need)

  • Feature: service-start package plus operator onboarding for the pilot team.
  • Advantage: trained operators and a defined support path from day one, not after something breaks.
  • Benefit: continuity through the pilot and a clear answer to “who responds when a unit fails.” Maps to: “Training and response time matter almost as much as the machine itself.”
  • Check: “Does a defined response and onboarding plan give you enough confidence to start at one site?”

Chain 3 — Economy / defendable scope (commercial logic, figures to confirm)

  • Feature: one standardized fleet with a bundled, written service scope.
  • Advantage: a single operating model instead of local workarounds, comparable on more than unit price.
  • Benefit: a scope procurement can defend internally — value visible, not assumed. Maps to: “We can justify the pilot if performance and service reliability are visible quickly.”
  • Check: “What does the commercial review need to see for the service component to stand on its own?”

Role variants

For Lars Meijer (Operations)

Lead with Chain 1 (uptime) and close on rollout logic: prove disruption drops at one site, then scale. Keep service as the reason it stays reliable, not a separate topic.

For Sophie Verbeek (Procurement)

Lead with Chain 3 (defendable scope) and a total-cost-of-ownership view: standardization plus service against avoidable-downtime cost — not a unit-price line. Carry any figure as to-verify until the YelloFile and a real quote confirm it.

What was deliberately left out

  • Comfort, Safety, and Image chains — the need profile rates these underexplored. They are not argued here; they stay on the follow-up list until the evidence is in.
  • Any hard cost or performance number not yet confirmed from the YelloFile or a quote.

NorthStar Demo Storyboard

📄 Open the live Google Doc ↗

Simulated Demo Gem output, built from the NorthStar Value Block and the BR 45/22 YelloFile. It plans an on-site demonstration as an ordered storyboard: each step ties to a confirmed need, carries a short talk-track line and the most likely objection, and ends with a setup checklist. Show the representative hardest case on the customer’s own floor, not a staged one. Product facts and media come from the YelloFile; every site-specific kit item is flagged to-confirm.

Demo goal: make the two confirmed strong needs — uptime and service continuity — visible in ten minutes on NorthStar’s own site.

Storyboard

Step 1 — The hardest real case (need: Performance / uptime)

  • Show: the BR 45/22 C Bp Pack running the dirtiest, busiest stretch of floor at a shift-change moment.
  • Talk-track: “This is the window you said hurts most — let’s clean it the way a normal shift would, not a staged lane.”
  • Likely objection: “Our floors are worse than a demo lane.” → That is exactly why we run it on your floor, at your hardest spot.

Step 2 — Run continuity (need: Performance / uptime)

  • Show: battery runtime across the representative area without a mid-task stop; swap/charge logic (runtime figure from YelloFile — to-verify).
  • Talk-track: “No trailing cable, consistent power — the point is finishing the window without an interruption.”
  • Likely objection: “What happens when the battery runs low mid-shift?” → Walk the swap routine; this is where onboarding matters.

Step 3 — The service and onboarding picture (need: Service / continuity)

  • Show: the onboarding steps an operator actually does, and the service-start / escalation path on one page.
  • Talk-track: “If a unit goes down, this is who responds and how fast — service is part of the machine, not a separate conversation.”
  • Likely objection: “Service promises sound generic.” → Point to the written response path, not a slogan.

Step 4 — The decision frame (need: Economy / defendable scope)

  • Show: the pilot scope on one slide — three units, onboarding, service-start — and the single review date.
  • Talk-track: “Prove it here, then decide on a rollout. One site, one clear result.”
  • Likely objection: “It’s still just three machines.” → Yes — that is the low-risk point of a pilot.

Setup checklist

  • Three BR 45/22 C Bp Pack units, charged, with spare battery (confirm battery/charger kit from YelloFile).
  • Access to the representative hardest floor area agreed with Milan in advance — to-confirm.
  • On-site water, power, and surface conditions suitable for the units — to-confirm.
  • Operator (Milan’s team) available to try it, not just watch — to-confirm.
  • One-page service / escalation sheet printed; YelloFile open for any spec question.
  • Safety walk with Eva before running on a live floor — to-confirm.

NorthStar Meeting Summary

📄 Open the live Google Doc ↗

Simulated Closing-Persona Gem output (summary mode), built from rough notes after the NorthStar closing conversation with procurement. The Gem keeps agreed and open strictly separate and flags anything unclear rather than filling it in. This is the artifact you attach in follow-up.

Conversation: close-stage discussion with Sophie Verbeek (Procurement) on the three-unit pilot. Signal read: engaged with the pilot, but kept returning to the quote and asked to compare on unit price only; no timeline yet.

Agreed

  • The pilot scope is understood: three BR 45/22 C Bp Pack scrubber driers, operator onboarding, and a service-start package.
